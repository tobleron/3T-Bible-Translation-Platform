## Step 3: Comprehensive Interpretation Report

### Biblical Context
This passage presents a genealogy of Jesus Christ tracing His lineage back to Abraham through King David. The structure of the genealogy divides it into three segments of fourteen generations each:

1. From Abraham to David (14 generations)
2. From David to the Babylonian exile (14 generations)
3. From the Babylonian exile to Christ (14 generations)

This structure is significant for several reasons:
- It emphasizes Jesus' connection to the covenant promises made to Abraham and David.
- The number thirteen was considered unlucky in Jewish tradition, making fourteen a symbol of completeness and divine blessing.
- The inclusion of women like Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, and others highlights God's grace extended beyond traditional patriarchal lines.

### Theological Significance
The genealogy serves multiple theological purposes:
1. **Divine Plan:** It shows that Jesus is part of God's redemptive plan, fulfilling Old Testament prophecies about the Messiah coming from the line of Abraham and David.
2. **Inclusivity:** The presence of Gentile women (Rahab and Ruth) and individuals with morally ambiguous backgrounds (Tamar, Bathsheba) underscores God's grace toward all people.
3. **Redemption Through Sin:** The mention of Uriah's wife (Bathsheba) acknowledges the sinfulness of human lineage but also affirms God's ability to bring redemption through flawed humanity.

### Early Church Fathers' Interpretations
Early church fathers such as Irenaeus and Augustine emphasized the importance of this genealogy in confirming Jesus' divinity and His fulfillment of messianic prophecy. They saw it as evidence of Christ being both fully human and fully divine, bridging the gap between heaven and earth.
