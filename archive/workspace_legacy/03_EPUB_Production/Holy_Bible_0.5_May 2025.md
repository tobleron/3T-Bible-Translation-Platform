# Matthew
## Chapter 1
### The Genealogy of Jesus Christ
**1** The Genealogy of Jesus Christ, son of David, son of Abraham.
**2** Abraham fathered Isaac, Isaac fathered Jacob, Jacob fathered Judah and his brothers.
**3** Judah fathered Perez and Zerah by Tamar, Perez fathered Hezron, Hezron fathered Ram.
**4** Ram fathered Amminadab, Amminadab fathered Nahshon, Nahshon fathered Salmon.
**5** Salmon fathered Boaz by Rahab, Boaz fathered Obed by Ruth, Obed fathered Jesse.
**6** Jesse fathered David the king. David fathered Solomon by the wife of Uriah.
**7** Solomon fathered Rehoboam, Rehoboam fathered Abijah, Abijah fathered Asa.
**8** Asa fathered Jehoshaphat, Jehoshaphat fathered Joram, Joram fathered Uzziah.
**9** Uzziah fathered Jotham, Jotham fathered Ahaz, Ahaz fathered Hezekiah.
**10** Hezekiah fathered Manasseh, Manasseh fathered Ammon, Ammon fathered Josiah.
**11** Josiah fathered Jeconiah and his brothers during the Babylonian exile.
**12** After the Babylonian exile, Jeconiah fathered Shealtiel, Shealtiel fathered Zerubbabel.
**13** Zerubbabel fathered Abiud, Abiud fathered Eliakim, Eliakim fathered Azor.
**14** Azor fathered Zadok, Zadok fathered Achim, Achim fathered Eliud.
**15** Eliud fathered Eleazar, Eleazar fathered Matthan, Matthan fathered Jacob.
**16** Jacob fathered Joseph, the husband of Mary, of whom Jesus was born, who is called Christ.
**17** Thus from Abraham to David there were fourteen generations; from David to the Babylonian exile, fourteen generations; and from the Babylonian exile to Christ, fourteen generations.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-1-17-a'>a</a></sup>
### The Birth of Jesus Christ
**18** This is how the birth of Jesus Christ came about: His mother Mary, legally engaged to Joseph, was found to have a child in her womb from the Holy Spirit before they joined together.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-1-18-b'>b</a></sup>
**19** Now Joseph her husband, being a righteous man and not wanting to disgrace her publicly, decided to send her away secretly.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-1-19-c'>c</a></sup>
**20** As he pondered these things, an angel of the Lord appeared in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for the child conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.”
**21** She will bear a son, and you will call his name Jesus, for he will save his people from their sins.
**22** All this happened to fulfill what the Lord had spoken through the prophet, saying,
**23** “Behold, the virgin in her womb will conceive and bear a son and they will call his name Immanuel”, which means ‘With us is God’.
**24** When Joseph awoke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him and took Mary as his wife.
**25** But he did not touch her until she gave birth to a son, and he called his name Jesus.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-1-25-d'>d</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 1:17* - a. Matthew arranges the genealogy in three groups of fourteen generations (Abraham–David; David–Babylonian exile; exile–Christ) for mnemonic and symbolic reasons. The number 14 corresponds to the numeric value of *David* in Hebrew <i>דוד = 4+6+4</i>. He intentionally omits several kings (e.g., Ahaziah, Joash, Amaziah from v. 8) to shape the structure, emphasizing Jesus as the promised son of David and signaling divine design in Israel’s history (cf. 2 Chronicles 22–25). This stylized list follows ancient Jewish literary practices for theological storytelling.
*Matthew 1:18* - b. The phrase ‘from the Holy Spirit’ translates the Greek <i>ek pneumatos hagiou</i>, which emphasizes origin rather than association. It points to the conception as truly originating in the Spirit's initiating action—not merely attributed to the Spirit (“of the Holy Spirit”) or performed as an external act (“by the Holy Spirit”), but proceeding directly from the divine source.
*Matthew 1:19* - c. The term ‘disgrace’ reflects the severe social and moral consequences in first-century Jewish culture, where public shame was devastating. The Greek <i>deigmatisai</i> implies exposing her publicly, while ‘send her away quietly’ translates <i>apolusai</i> (to release or dismiss). Together, they reveal Joseph’s dilemma and his desire to protect Mary’s dignity with quiet compassion.
*Matthew 1:25* - d. The omission of ‘firstborn’ aligns with the earliest and most reliable manuscripts, avoiding potential ambiguity about Mary’s marital status after Jesus’ birth. While later versions include it, this may imply additional children—a theological concern for traditions emphasizing Mary’s perpetual virginity. However, the word ‘until’ is retained, reflecting the Greek <i>heōs hou</i>, which denotes a state up to a specific point without implying change afterward. The phrase ‘did not touch her’ replaces the traditional rendering ‘did not know her’ to avoid ambiguity about sexual union, preserving clarity and poetic tone while adhering to biblical idiom. The Greek <i>egnō</i> refers to intimate knowledge in this context.
## Chapter 2
### The Magi Visit Herod
**1** After Jesus was born in Bethlehem of Judea during the days of Herod the king, magi from the east came to Jerusalem, saying, &#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-2-1-a'>a</a></sup>
**2** “Where is the one who has been born King of the Jews? We saw his star in the east and have come to worship him.”
**3** When King Herod heard this, he was troubled, and all Jerusalem with him.
**4** He gathered the chief priests and <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a> of the people and inquired from them where the Christ was to be born.
**5** “In Bethlehem of Judea,” they replied, “for so it is written by the prophet:
**6** ‘And you, Bethlehem, in Judah, are by no means least among the rulers of Judah, for from you will come a ruler who will shepherd my people Israel.’”
**7** Then Herod secretly called the magi and inquired from them exactly the time the star had appeared.
**8** He sent them to Bethlehem and said, “Go, search carefully for the child. When you find him, report to me so that I also may come and worship him.”
**9** After hearing the king, they went their way; and behold, the star they had seen in the east now led them until it stopped above the place where the child was.
**10** When they saw the star, they rejoiced immensely with overwhelming joy.
**11** Entering the house, they saw the child with his mother Mary, and bowing down they worshippied him. Then they opened their treasures and presented him with gifts of gold, frankincense, and myrrh.
**12** Being warned in a dream not to return to Herod, they departed to their homeland by another route.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-2-12-b'>b</a></sup>
### Joseph’s Refuge In Egypt
**13** After they departed, an angel of the Lord appeared to Joseph in a dream, saying, “Rise, take the child and his mother, flee to Egypt, and remain there until I speak to you. Herod is about to pursue the child to destroy him.”
**14** So he rose, took the child and his mother by night, and withdrew into Egypt.
**15** He remained there until Herod died, to fulfill what the Lord had spoken through the prophet saying, ‘Out of Egypt I called my son.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-2-15-c'>c</a></sup>
### The Weeping of Rachel
**16** When Herod construed that he was mocked by the magi, he was deeply enraged and ordered the massacre of all the male children two years old and under in Bethlehem and its surrounding region; based on the exact timing he had inquired from the magi.
**17** Then was fulfilled what was spoken through the prophet Jeremiah, saying,
**18** ‘A voice was heard in Ramah, weeping and deep mourning, Rachel weeping for her children and she refused to be comforted, because they are no more.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-2-18-d'>d</a></sup>
### The Humble Nazarene
**19** After Herod had died, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to Joseph in Egypt in a dream, saying,
**20** “Rise, take the child and his mother, go into the land of Israel, for those who persued the child’s life are now dead.”
**21** So he rose, took the child and his mother, and entered into the land of Israel.
**22** But when he heard Archelaus ruled Judea in place of his father Herod, he was afraid to go there. After being warned in a dream, he withdrew to the region of Galilee,
**23** and settled in a town called Nazareth, to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet: ‘He will be called a Nazarene.’

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 2:1* - a. Magi refers to learned individuals from the east, often associated with Zoroastrian traditions or astronomical knowledge.
*Matthew 2:12* - b. The phrase 'by another route' emphasizes the Magi's deliberate caution to avoid detection by Herod, reflecting the danger implied in the warning.
*Matthew 2:15* - c. 'Out of Egypt I called my son' (Hosea 11:1) originally referred to Israel’s deliverance from Egypt under Moses. Matthew applies it typologically to Jesus, framing Him as the new Israel, fulfilling God’s redemptive plan.
*Matthew 2:18* - d. Rachel was a beloved wife of Jacob from Genesis (Gen 29–30), who died giving birth to Benjamin and is traditionally believed to be buried near Bethlehem at Ramah or Rama. In Jewish tradition, she became a symbolic figure of mourning for the nation of Israel, especially during times of exile and suffering. The quotation from Jeremiah 31:15 ('A voice was heard in Ramah… Rachel weeping for her children') is used anachronistically—applying a prophecy from the Babylonian exile (circa 6th century BCE) to Herod’s massacre (circa 1st century BCE)—to highlight the sorrow of God over the death of innocent children and to show how Jesus’ life fulfills ancient prophecies.
## Chapter 3
### John Prepares The Way
**1** In those days John the Baptist appeared, proclaiming in the wilderness of Judea, saying,&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-1-a'>a</a></sup>
**2** “Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near.”
**3** For this is the one spoken of by the prophet Isaiah, saying, “A voice of one crying out in the wilderness, ‘Prepare the way of the Lord, make His paths straight.’”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-3-b'>b</a></sup>
**4** Now John himself had camel-hair clothing, an animal skin belt around his waist, and his food was locusts and wild honey.
**5** Jerusalem, all Judea, and the whole Jordan region went out to him,&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-5-c'>c</a></sup>
**6** and they were being baptized by him in the Jordan River, confessing openly their sins.
**7** But when he saw many Pharisees and Sadducees coming for his baptism, he said, “You brood of vipers! Who warned you to flee from the coming wrath?”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-7-d'>d</a></sup>
**8** Bear fruit, therefore, worthy of repentance.
**9** Do not suppose in your thinking, “We have Abraham as our father,” for I tell you that God is able to bring to life children for Abraham out of these stones.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-9-e'>e</a></sup>
**10** The axe is laid at the root of the trees already; and every tree that does not bear good fruit will be cut down and thrown into the fire.
**11** I baptize you with water for repentance, but the one who comes after me is mightier than I, whose sandals I am not worthy to carry. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-11-f'>f</a></sup>
**12** His winnowing shovel is in his hand. He will purge his threshing floor, gather his wheat into the storehouse, and burn the chaff with unquenchable fire.
### The Baptism of Jesus
**13** Then Jesus came from Galilee to the Jordan to be baptized by John.
**14** But John tried to prevent him, saying, “I need to be baptized by you, yet you come to me?”
**15** Jesus answered, “Allow it for now, for it is fitting for us to fulfill all <a href="glossary.xhtml#righteousness" class="glossary-word">righteousness</a>.” Then John allowed him.
**16** As soon as Jesus rose from the waters of baptism, the heavens opened, and he saw the Spirit of God descending like a dove coming to rest upon him.
**17** Behold, a voice came from heaven saying, “This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-3-17-g'>g</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 3:1* - a. The phrase 'John the Baptist appeared, proclaiming...' establishes his role as the prophetic herald (cf. Malachi 3:1).
*Matthew 3:3* - b. The quote from Isaiah 40:3 (LXX) emphasizes the fulfillment of messianic prophecy, with 'way of the Lord' highlighting divine sovereignty.
*Matthew 3:5* - c. The phrase 'Jordan region' reflects the geographical focus of the narrative, emphasizing the scope of John’s impact.
*Matthew 3:7* - d. The metaphor 'brood of vipers' <i>genne echidnōn</i> underscores the hypocrisy of the Pharisees and Sadducees (cf. Matthew 12:34).
*Matthew 3:9* - e. John challenges reliance on physical lineage (Abraham) over true spiritual transformation (cf. Luke 3:8).
*Matthew 3:11* - f. The 'Holy Spirit and fire' likely symbolize Jesus' dual role as purifier and judge (cf. Malachi 3:2–3).
*Matthew 3:17* - g. The voice from heaven affirms Jesus’ identity as the Son of God, aligning with Psalm 2:7 and the Trinitarian structure of baptism (cf. Matthew 28:19).
## Chapter 4
### The Devil Tempts Jesus
**1** Then Jesus was led by the Spirit into the wilderness to be tempted by the devil.
**2** After fasting forty days and forty nights, he became hungry.
**3** The tempter approached and said to him: “If you are the Son of God, command these stones to become bread.”
**4** Jesus answered, “It is written: ‘Man will not live on bread alone, but on every word coming from the mouth of God.’”
**5** Then the devil took him to the holy city and set him on the pinnacle of the temple.
**6** “If you are the Son of God,” he said, “throw yourself down, for it is written: ‘He will command His angels concerning you,’ and ‘on their hands they will lift you up lest you strike your foot against a stone.’”
**7** Jesus answered, “It is also written: ‘You are not to put the Lord your God to the test.’”
**8** Again the devil took him to a very high mountain, showed him all the kingdoms of the world and their splendor.
**9** He said to him: “All these I will give to you if bowing down you worship me.”
**10** Jesus replied, “Go away Satan! For it is written: ‘You will worship the Lord your God, and Him only you will serve.’”
**11** Then the devil departed from him, and angels approached and served him.
### The Rising Light Prophecy
**12** When Jesus heard that John had been arrested, he withdrew into Galilee.
**13** He left Nazareth and settled in Capernaum by the sea, around the borders of Zebulun and Naphtali.
**14** To fulfill what was spoken through the prophet Isaiah saying,
**15** “Land of Zebulun and land of Naphtali, way of the sea, beyond the Jordan, Galilee of the <a href="glossary.xhtml#gentiles" class="glossary-word">Gentiles</a>.
**16** The people sitting in darkness have seen a great light; on those sitting in the shadow of death, a light has dawned.”
### The Master’s Chosen
**17** Since then Jesus began to preach saying, “Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near.”
**18** Walking by the Sea of Galilee, he saw two brothers: Simon (the one called Peter), and Andrew his brother, casting a net into the sea, for they were fishermen.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-4-18-a'>a</a></sup>
**19** He said to them: “Come, follow me, and I will make you fishers of humans.”
**20** At once, they left their nets and followed him.
**21** Going further, he saw two brothers, James the son of Zebedee and his brother John, in the boat with their father Zebedee, mending their nets and he called them.
**22** At once, they left the boat and their father and followed him.
**23** He was going around Galilee, teaching in their synagogues, proclaiming the gospel of the kingdom, and healing every disease and sickness in the people.
**24** His fame spread throughout Syria, and they brought to him all the sick, those afflicted by various diseases and torments, the demon-possessed, lunatics, and paralytics, and he healed them.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-4-24-b'>b</a></sup>
**25** Large crowds followed him from Galilee, the Decapolis, Jerusalem, Judea, and beyond the Jordan.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-4-25-c'>c</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 4:18* - a. The name 'Peter' (Cephas in Aramaic) means 'rock' and is a new identity given to Simon by Jesus (cf. Matthew 16:18). This name change symbolizes his role as a foundational figure in God’s kingdom.
*Matthew 4:24* - b. 'Demon-possessed, epileptics, and paralytics' reflect the breadth of Jesus’ healing ministry in first-century society. These conditions were often associated with spiritual or physical impurity, and His miracles demonstrated authority over both.
*Matthew 4:25* - c. The Decapolis was a Gentile region beyond the Jordan, highlighting the inclusivity of Jesus’ ministry reaching diverse groups in Galilee, Judea, and beyond.
## Chapter 5
### The Beatitudes
**1** Seeing the crowds, Jesus went up the mountain. When he sat down, his disciples came to him.
**2** He opened his mouth and began to teach them, saying,
**3** “Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.
**4** Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted.
**5** Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-5-a'>a</a></sup>
**6** Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for <a href="glossary.xhtml#righteousness" class="glossary-word">righteousness</a>, for they will be fulfilled.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-6-b'>b</a></sup>
**7** Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy.
**8** Blessed are the pure in heart, for they will see God.
**9** Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called sons of God.
**10** Blessed are those persecuted for the sake of <a href="glossary.xhtml#righteousness" class="glossary-word">righteousness</a>, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.
**11** Blessed are you when people revile you, persecute you, and speak all kinds of evil against you because of me.
**12** Rejoice and exult, for great is your reward in heaven because in the same way they persecuted the prophets before you.”
### Salt and Light
**13** “You are the salt of the earth, but if the salt dulls out, what will restore its saltiness? It is good for nothing but to be thrown out and trampled underfoot by people.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-13-c'>c</a></sup>
**14** You are the light of the world. A city set on a hill cannot be hidden.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-14-d'>d</a></sup>
**15** No one lights a lamp and puts it under a basket, but on a lampstand, and it shines for all who are in the house.
**16** Therefore, let your light shine before people, so they may see your good works and glorify your Father who is in heaven.”
### Christ Fulfills the Law
**17** Do not think I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets. I have not come to abolish them, but to fulfill them.
**18** Amen I tell you, until heaven and earth pass away, not one stroke or one letter will pass away from the law, until all things are accomplished.
**19** Therefore, whoever breaks one of the least of these commandments and teaches others to do the same will be called least in the kingdom of heaven. But whoever does and teaches them, this one will be called great in the kingdom of heaven.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-19-e'>e</a></sup>
**20** I tell you, unless your <a href="glossary.xhtml#righteousness" class="glossary-word">righteousness</a> surpasses that of the <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a> and Pharisees, you will by no means enter the kingdom of heaven.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-20-f'>f</a></sup>
### Anger and Reconciliation
**21** You have heard it said to the ancients: “Do not murder. Whoever murders will be liable to judgment.”
**22** But I tell you, whoever is angry with a brother in faith is liable to judgment. Whoever says to a sister in faith ‘You Fool!’ is liable to the court, and whoever says, ‘You godless one!’ is liable to the fire of <a href="glossary.xhtml#gehenna" class="glossary-word">Gehenna</a>.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-22-g'>g</a></sup>
**23** If you are offering your gift at the altar and remember that a brother or sister in faith holds something against you,
**24** leave your gift there, go be reconciled with them, then come offer your gift.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-24-h'>h</a></sup>
**25** Settle quickly with your accuser while you are on the way, lest they hand you to the judge, and the judge to the officer, and you will be thrown into prison.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-25-i'>i</a></sup>
**26** Amen I tell you, you will by no means get out until you have paid the last quadrans.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-26-j'>j</a></sup>
### Lust and Inner Purity
**27** You have heard that it was said, “Do not commit adultery.”
**28** But I tell you, anyone who looks at a woman to lust for her has already committed adultery with her in his heart.
**29** If your right eye causes you to sin, pluck it out and throw it away from you. For it is better for you to lose one part of your body than for your whole body to be thrown into <a href="glossary.xhtml#gehenna" class="glossary-word">Gehenna</a>.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-29-k'>k</a></sup>
**30** And if your right hand causes you to sin, cut it off and throw it away from you. For it is better for you to lose one part of your body than for your whole body to perish in <a href="glossary.xhtml#gehenna" class="glossary-word">Gehenna</a>.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-30-l'>l</a></sup>
### On Divorce and Oaths
**31** “It was said, ‘Any man who divorces his wife must give her a certificate of divorce.’
**32** But I tell you, any man who divorces his wife except for sexual covenant treachery makes her commit adultery. And any man who marries a divorced woman commits adultery.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-32-m'>m</a></sup>
**33** You have heard it said to the ancients, “Do not swear falsely, but fulfill your oaths to the Lord.
**34** But I tell you, do not swear at all, either by heaven, for it is the throne of God;
**35** or by the earth, for it is the footstool of His feet; or by Jerusalem, for it is the city of the Great King.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-35-n'>n</a></sup>
**36** And do not swear by your head, because you cannot make one hair white or black.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-36-o'>o</a></sup>
**37** Let your final word be ‘Yes’ or ‘No’ for anything more comes from the evil one.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-37-p'>p</a></sup>
### On Retaliation and Unconditional Love
**38** “You have heard that it was said, ‘An eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth.’
**39** But I tell you, do not resist evil; whoever slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other also.
**40** If anyone sues you to have your tunic, give them your cloak also.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-40-q'>q</a></sup>
**41** If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-41-r'>r</a></sup>
**42** Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you.
**43** You have heard that it was said, ‘Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-43-s'>s</a></sup>
**44** But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you.
**45** So you may become the children of your Father who is in heaven, because he makes his sun rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous.
**46** For if you love those who love you, what reward will you have? Do not even tax-collectors do the same?&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-46-t'>t</a></sup>
**47** If you greet only your own, what extraordinary thing are you doing? Do not even the <a href="glossary.xhtml#gentiles" class="glossary-word">Gentiles</a> do the same?&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-5-47-u'>u</a></sup>
**48** Therefore, be perfect as your heavenly Father is perfect.”

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 5:5* - a. Greek <i>gē</i> can refer to the land (as in the promised land of Israel) or to the whole earth, depending on context. Jesus draws from Psalm 37 (especially vv. 9, 11, 29), where the meek are promised inheritance of the land as a covenant blessing. In this Beatitude, He reinterprets the promise universally: the meek—those who submit to God—will inherit the earth in the coming kingdom, fulfilling and surpassing Old Testament expectations.
*Matthew 5:6* - b. The word “fulfilled” was chosen over the traditional “filled” to reflect the unmistakably spiritual nature of Jesus' promise. The Greek verb <i>chortazō</i> refers to being fed or satisfied, but in this context, it transcends physical nourishment and speaks to the deep spiritual hunger for righteousness. The hunger and thirst described here are not bodily cravings, but inner longings for God's justice, holiness, and presence to be made real in the soul and in the world. While “filled” might suggest momentary relief, “fulfilled” communicates a lasting, transformative completion. This aligns with the biblical theme of God satisfying the righteous not with food or drink, but with truth, restoration, and divine presence (cf. Isaiah 55:1–2; Psalm 107:9). This rendering captures the eschatological and spiritual intent behind Jesus' words: not temporary comfort, but the ultimate realization of righteousness in the life of the believer and in the kingdom to come.
*Matthew 5:13* - c. Salt in ancient times was valued primarily for its ability to preserve—not to flavor. Jesus uses this image to reveal the disciple's calling: to slow the world's moral decay and uphold the integrity of God's truth amid corruption. The phrase “loses its preserving power” conveys this responsibility more clearly than “loses its flavor,” which risks reducing the metaphor to mere taste. In Jesus' view, a disciple who no longer preserves truth becomes spiritually useless—cast out, not for lack of passion, but for lack of purpose.
*Matthew 5:14* - d. The word “shines” was chosen instead of “gives light” to preserve both the poetic weight and the original intent of the metaphor. The Greek verb <i>lampei</i> means “to shine” or “to beam forth.” While “gives light” emphasizes the function of a lamp, it lacks the spiritual resonance intended in Jesus' metaphor. He was not merely describing usefulness, but radiance—one who lives in truth, revealing the divine through their very presence. “Shines” conveys purity, visibility, and the natural outflow of a transformed life. It evokes the image of a soul that illuminates its surroundings not merely by purpose, but by nature.
*Matthew 5:19* - e. The word “breaks” translates the Greek verb <i>lyō</i>, meaning “to loosen” or “to release.” In legal contexts, it came to mean relaxing or violating a commandment—whether by dismissing its importance or undermining its binding force. Jesus warns not only against defying God's commandments but also against treating even the least of them as negligible, especially when teaching others. The phrase “lives by them” reflects the Greek verb <i>poieō</i>, often rendered “to do” or “to practice.” Here, it implies more than surface-level obedience; it suggests a life genuinely shaped by God's will. The contrast sets a clear standard: greatness in the kingdom is measured not by knowledge, but by wholehearted practice and faithful instruction.
*Matthew 5:20* - f. The scribes were educated experts in the Jewish Law (Torah), responsible for copying, interpreting, and teaching Scripture. They held influential religious roles, often aligned with the Pharisees, and were viewed as the spiritual authorities of their time. However, Jesus frequently challenged their outward piety and legalism, exposing a lack of inward righteousness.
*Matthew 5:22* - g. This translation more faithfully reflects the Greek structure and the rising severity of Jesus' warning. “Worthless” renders the Aramaic <i>raca</i>, a culturally charged insult implying emptiness or contempt. While many versions retain <i>Raca</i> untranslated, we clarify its meaning for modern readers to preserve its original sting. The second insult in the verse, “you godless one,” translates the Greek word <i>moros</i>. This is not a simple attack on intelligence but a spiritually escalated accusation—denoting one who is morally perverse or spiritually rebellious toward God, as echoed in Psalm 14:1: “The fool says in his heart, ‘There is no God.’”
*Matthew 5:24* - h. The phrase “to God” is added at the end of the verse for the sake of modern clarity, even though it does not appear explicitly in the original Greek. In the context of first-century Judaism, presenting a gift at the altar clearly and universally implied an offering to God, and no further specification was needed. However, in modern English—especially for readers unfamiliar with ancient sacrificial practices—the word “offering” may seem vague or transactional. Adding “to God” clarifies the purpose without altering the meaning.
*Matthew 5:25* - i. The phrase “to court” is added for clarity, though it does not appear explicitly in the Greek. The original expression, using the word <i>hōdō</i> (meaning “on the way”), reads “while you are on the way with them,” but the context—adversary, judge, officer, and prison—clearly implies a legal proceeding. In first-century Jewish culture, it was common for disputes to be resolved privately while traveling to court, often to avoid harsher legal outcomes. This addition helps modern readers recognize that the warning is not about casual disagreement, but about settling a legal or moral conflict before formal judgment is passed. It preserves the original meaning while making the implied legal setting explicit.
*Matthew 5:26* - j. The quadrans was the smallest Roman copper coin, worth one-fourth of an as (a basic unit of Roman currency) and approximately 1/64th of a denarius (a day’s wage for a laborer). By referencing this minuscule denomination, Jesus underscores the necessity of resolving even the most trivial debts or disputes before facing divine judgment, highlighting the importance of moral integrity and urgency in reconciliation (cf. 2 Chronicles 22–25 for broader themes of accountability). The metaphor stresses completeness and finality: until every obligation—no matter how small—is settled, one remains under the judgment of the court (or God’s justice).
*Matthew 5:29* - k. Jesus' command to “pluck out” the eye is not meant literally but reflects a well-known rabbinic metaphor highlighting the radical seriousness of sin. The “eye” symbolizes a gateway of temptation, and the call to remove it dramatizes the need to cut off sin at its root—even if it feels essential or deeply ingrained. This hyperbolic language is meant to awaken the conscience, underscoring that no personal cost is too great if it protects the soul from spiritual ruin. The phrase “thrown into <i>Gehenna</i> (torment of spiritual desolation)” refers to the Greek word <i>geenna</i>, originally linked to the Valley of Hinnom—a site of prophetic judgment and condemnation (cf. Jeremiah 7:31). For a fuller theological and emotional exploration of what <i>Gehenna</i> represents, see the footnote on Matthew 5:22.
*Matthew 5:30* - l. This verse echoes the warning of verse 29, using hyperbolic language to emphasize the urgency of cutting off sin at its source. The Greek verb <i>blēthēnai</i> (“to be thrown”) conveys a decisive and forceful removal—not a gentle departure, but the severity of divine judgment. The phrase “thrown into <i>Gehenna</i> (torment of spiritual desolation)” expresses not physical torment but spiritual exile—the soul's separation from the Source of life. <i>Gehenna</i> represents ultimate spiritual ruin: the place of severed relationship with God. For a fuller theological and emotional exploration of what <i>Gehenna</i> signifies, see the footnote on Matthew 5:22.
*Matthew 5:32* - m. The Greek word <i>porneia</i> is often rendered “sexual immorality,” “unchastity,” or “fornication.” However, such translations can sound either vague or culturally disconnected from the biblical context. The phrase “sexual covenant treachery” was chosen to highlight the heart of what <i>porneia</i> implies within the framework of marriage: a willful betrayal of the sacred covenant through sexual unfaithfulness. It captures both the physical act and the spiritual violation, emphasizing not just misconduct, but the relational breach that makes divorce permissible in Jesus' teaching. While more interpretive than “whoredom” or “sexual immorality,” this phrase conveys the theological and emotional significance that best reflects the intent of the original term.
*Matthew 5:35* - n. The term “footstool,” from the Greek <i>hupopodion</i>, refers to a low platform on which a ruler would rest his feet while seated on a throne. In biblical language, it symbolizes dominion and subordination. Jesus echoes Isaiah 66:1, where God declares, “Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool,” affirming his sovereign rule over creation. The phrase “the great King,” from the Greek <i>tou megalou basileōs</i>, is drawn from Psalm 48:2, referring to YHWH, whose presence sanctified Jerusalem as his royal city. By calling it “the city of the great King,” Jesus underscores that even swearing by sacred places is ultimately swearing by God himself, making oath-taking an inescapably divine matter.
*Matthew 5:36* - o. Jesus draws attention to something as ordinary as the color of hair to reveal something extraordinary about human limitation. The Greek emphasizes inability—“you are not able” (<i>ou dynasai</i>)—to change even the smallest detail of your own being. In biblical thought, hair often symbolizes identity or divine distinction (cf. Luke 12:7, “even the hairs of your head are all numbered”). By saying one cannot make a single hair white or black, Jesus underscores that we do not even possess full command over our own bodies—let alone the moral or spiritual weight to guarantee truth through an oath. Swearing “by your head,” then, is not only ineffective but presumptuous. The deeper message is that truth does not draw its credibility from human authority, but from alignment with divine reality—where one's word stands simply because it is true.
*Matthew 5:37* - p. The phrase “Yes for yes” and “No for no” reflects the intended meaning of the Greek idiom <i>nai nai, ou ou</i>, which emphasizes sincerity and clarity in speech. Rather than prescribing a ritual formula, Jesus is calling for truthfulness that stands without the need for oaths or exaggerated assurances. In first-century usage, the repetition of <i>nai</i> (yes) or <i>ou</i> (no) served to affirm one's words as trustworthy and resolute. This rendering preserves the spirit of Jesus' teaching—that integrity requires no external reinforcement—while helping modern readers recognize that genuine affirmation or denial should be sufficient for a disciple of the kingdom.
*Matthew 5:40* - q. Jesus' command to give up even one's cloak goes beyond legal obligation, modeling a radical posture of non-retaliation and voluntary self-sacrifice. This hyperbolic instruction is not about enabling injustice but about exposing it—disarming the oppressor by surrendering what they demand and refusing to enter the power struggle. It teaches that spiritual integrity is more powerful than personal rights.
*Matthew 5:41* - r. Roman authorities could legally require civilians to carry burdens for a mile. Jesus teaches going beyond what is required.
*Matthew 5:43* - s. The command to 'love your neighbor' comes directly from Leviticus 19:18 and is rightly attributed to the Law. However, the phrase “and will hate your enemy” does not appear in the Old Testament. It reflects a common interpretive tradition in Second Temple Judaism, likely influenced by Israel's historical posture toward national enemies and reinforced by certain imprecatory psalms. Some sectarian groups, such as the Qumran community (Dead Sea Scrolls), explicitly taught to “love the sons of light and hate the sons of darkness.” Jesus challenges this distortion by expanding the command of love beyond tribal, ethnic, and religious boundaries. His correction restores the true spirit of the Law, revealing that love is not reserved for allies but is to be extended even to enemies—those who oppose, injure, or persecute us.
*Matthew 5:46* - t. In first-century Jewish society, tax collectors were widely despised as collaborators with the Roman occupiers and were often associated with corruption and extortion. Jesus invokes them here not to condone their behavior, but to make a sharp contrast: even those considered morally compromised love those who love them. By comparing this to mere reciprocal affection, Jesus reveals that kingdom love must exceed common social behavior—it must be rooted in grace, not merit.
*Matthew 5:47* - u. The term “Gentiles” translates the Greek ethnē, referring to non-Jewish peoples. In this context, Jesus uses it not to demean other nations, but to emphasize that even those outside God's covenant with Israel naturally love those who love them. His point is that true righteousness goes beyond cultural norms and extends love even to enemies—mirroring the character of God Himself.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 6:2* - a. The phrase “sound a trumpet” is metaphorical, referring to showy, attention-seeking behavior. There is no evidence that literal trumpets were used in acts of charity. Jesus is condemning those who turn giving into public performance to gain praise. The phrase “they have their reward in full” translates the Greek <i>apechousin ton misthon autōn</i>; the verb <i>apechō</i> was used in financial contexts to indicate full payment received—meaning public praise is the only reward they will get, with nothing further from God.
*Matthew 6:6* - b. The Greek phrase ho blepōn en tō kryptō means “the one who sees in secret.” The words “what is done” were added for clarity, making the implied action explicit in the phrase “who sees what is done in secret.”
*Matthew 6:7* - c. The phrase “meaningless repetition” translates the Greek verb <i>battalogēsēte</i>, a rare term possibly derived from the onomatopoeic root <i>battal-</i>, suggesting stammering or babbling. While the precise etymology is uncertain, the meaning in context is clear: speaking with excessive, empty, or mechanical repetition. Jesus is not condemning repeated prayer—as seen in the Psalms or in his own prayers—but is warning against ritualistic or performance-driven speech, such as was common in pagan practices where word quantity was prioritized over heart sincerity. The expression “meaningless repetitions” captures this distinction with clarity for modern readers, avoiding confusion with genuine, heartfelt persistence in prayer.
*Matthew 6:13* - d. The concluding phrase, “For Yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever. Amen,” does not appear in the earliest Greek manuscripts of Matthew but is included in many later Byzantine texts and became part of the church's liturgical tradition. It was likely added during public worship and later incorporated into manuscript copies. While it may not have been spoken by Jesus in this specific context, the doxology is theologically consistent with the message of the prayer and serves as a fitting closure of praise. It is retained here for its deep devotional impact and its long-standing use in Christian worship, in keeping with this translation's emphasis on spiritual resonance and reader edification.
*Matthew 6:19* - e. “Pile up” renders <i>thēsaurizō</i> here negatively (greedy hoarding). It contrasts with the positive “store up … in heaven” in v. 20.
*Matthew 6:22* - f. The Greek word <i>haplous</i> refers to an eye that is single-minded, sincere, and morally undivided. It implies integrity of purpose—a clear, generous, and undistracted vision. This is not about physical health, but about ethical clarity: seeing the world through a heart free from greed, lust, or double motives. Jesus contrasts this with the “evil eye” in the next verse, a Hebrew idiom for selfishness or envy (cf. Proverbs 28:22). A “pure” eye lets in the light of truth; a corrupt eye darkens the whole self.
*Matthew 6:23* - g. The word “evil” translates the Greek <i>ponēros</i>, which refers to a morally and spiritually corrupt condition. Jesus is not merely speaking of sinful behavior, but of a distorted spiritual perception—when the “eye,” representing one's inner moral vision, is diseased. The result is that the entire person becomes darkened. The phrase “if the light you rely on is darkness” heightens the danger: it refers to those who believe they are enlightened, yet are profoundly self-deceived. The concluding cry, “how deep is that darkness,” is a Hebraic idiom expressing overwhelming moral blindness. It echoes Isaiah 5:20, where good is called evil and light is called darkness, underscoring the terrifying depth of spiritual delusion.
*Matthew 6:24* - h. Wealth here translates the Greek word <i>mamōnas</i> transliterated as <i>Mammon</i>, originally from Aramaic. It refers not merely to money or possessions but to wealth as a spiritual power—personified as a rival master to God. Jesus uses <i>Mammon</i> to expose the heart's divided loyalties, showing that true devotion cannot be shared between God and materialism.
*Matthew 6:25* - i. Jesus is not condemning planning or responsibility, but warning against anxious preoccupation with material needs. This verse follows his teaching that one cannot serve both God and wealth—the Greek word <i>mamōnas</i>, often personified to represent wealth as a spiritual rival. Worry, in this context, becomes a competing master—dividing the heart and pulling it away from trust in divine provision. By asking, “Is not life more than food…?” Jesus redirects attention to the God who gave life, affirming that the One who created the body can be trusted to sustain it. The command not to worry is not about passive detachment, but about active trust in a Father who knows and provides.
*Matthew 6:33* - j. "His righteousness" refers to God's moral standard, justice, and covenant faithfulness. To seek His righteousness means to pursue a life aligned with God's will—both in outward actions and inward devotion—reflecting what is right and pleasing to Him.
*Matthew 6:34* - k. Jesus teaches a posture of present-focused trust. “Tomorrow will carry its own burdens” means that future troubles will arrive in their time, and worrying now only adds unnecessary weight. Each day brings its own challenges; faith calls us to live one day at a time, trusting God for what lies ahead.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 7:5* - a. 'Dearest or dear one''s intentionally shifts from brother/sister to avoid over-literality while emphasizing relational accountability in the faith community.'
*Matthew 7:6* - b. The phrase “turn on you and tear you to pieces” comes from the Greek verbs <i>straphentes</i> (“to turn”) and <i>rhēxōsin</i> (“to tear apart”), painting a scene of sudden aggression. After rejecting what is holy, the pigs lash out at the giver. Jesus warns that offering sacred truth to those hostile or unreceptive may provoke backlash. The imagery of dogs and pigs—symbols of impurity in Jewish culture—underscores the need for discernment, not elitism, in sharing what is holy.
*Matthew 7:12* - c. The phrase “is foundational in the Law and the Prophets” reflects the Greek wording <i>houtos gar estin ho nomos kai hoi prophētai</i>, which literally means, “for this is the Law and the Prophets.” While some translations render this as “sums up,” such phrasing could mislead—since in Matthew 22:40, Jesus names love of God and neighbor as the two great commandments upon which all the Law and the Prophets depend. The Golden Rule captures the ethical essence of how we treat others, but not the full relational scope of Jesus' teaching. The phrase “foundational in” was chosen because it affirms the deep significance of this principle within the Hebrew Scriptures, while avoiding the implication that it alone captures the entirety of the Law's purpose. It reflects a core moral pillar without overstating its exclusivity.
*Matthew 7:23* - d. Jesus' words here express more than denial—they expose a relational void. “I never knew you” does not mean God lacked information about them, but that there was no intimacy, no communion of spirit. These were people who claimed His name but lived in contradiction to His will. To be called a “worker of lawlessness” is not just about sinful acts—it means living in persistent rebellion, outside the order and heart of God's kingdom. This is not rejection of sincere strugglers, but of those who practiced spiritual performance without transformation. The command to “depart” is the sorrowful justice of a Savior whose invitation was ignored until it was too late.
*Matthew 7:27* - e. This verse portrays the end of a life built without obedience to Jesus' teaching. The fall of the house represents the spiritual ruin that comes from hearing His word but not living by it. The contrast throughout the parable makes clear: only those who build on the foundation of His word—by doing it—will withstand the storms of judgment and life.
*Matthew 7:29* - f. The word <i>exousia</i> refers to intrinsic authority—not merely permission or delegated power, but divine right. Jesus' authority sharply contrasted with that of the scribes, who taught by citing rabbinic tradition. While they relied on precedent, He spoke with the weight of original, divine truth—as one who wrote the Law, not merely interpreted it. The crowd's astonishment was not merely intellectual but spiritual. They sensed the difference between inherited commentary and living revelation. Even His enemies later confessed, “No one ever spoke like this man” (John 7:46).

**33** The herdsmen fled and went into the town, reporting everything, including what had happened to the demon-possessed ones.
**34** Then, the whole town came out to meet Jesus. When they saw him, they entreated him to depart from their borders.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-8-34-f'>f</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 8:4* - a. Jesus' command, “See that you tell no one,” reflects what scholars call the Messianic Secret—a recurring pattern in the Gospels where Jesus intentionally downplays public recognition of his miracles. This wasn't due to timidity, but strategic timing: premature fame could distort his mission, incite political unrest, or provoke opposition before the appointed hour. The instruction to show himself to the priest aligns with Leviticus 14, fulfilling the law and offering the healing as testimony—not spectacle. Jesus honors both covenant protocol and divine discretion.
*Matthew 8:12* - b. The phrase “sons of the kingdom” refers to those—especially within Israel—who assumed their place in God's kingdom was guaranteed by ancestry or covenant identity, yet rejected Jesus as the Christ (cf. Matthew 21:43). The word “banished” translates the Greek <i>ekballō</i>, meaning “to throw out,” here signifying a solemn and final exclusion from God's presence. “Outer darkness” evokes a state of spiritual exile, cut off from the joy and light of the kingdom. The weeping and gnashing of teeth, repeated elsewhere (e.g. Matthew 13:42; 22:13), express profound regret and anguish. Jesus reverses expectations: those once assumed to be far off are welcomed in, while many presumed insiders are shut out—by their own unbelief, not divine injustice.
*Matthew 8:20* - c. The phrase ‘Son of Man’ is a messianic title rooted in Daniel 7:13, where one like a son of man is given authority by God. Jesus uses this title to reveal both His humanity and divine appointment. His saying contrasts the comfort of worldly homes with His own path of rejection and pilgrimage, emphasizing the cost of true discipleship.
*Matthew 8:22* - d. Jesus' command, “Let the dead bury their own dead,” is not a rejection of love or grief, but a call to radical spiritual urgency. The first “dead” refers to the spiritually unresponsive; the second, to the physically deceased. Though burial was a sacred duty in Jewish tradition, Jesus reveals that even the most honorable obligations must not delay obedience. His words expose the danger of postponing discipleship—even for reasons that seem noble.
*Matthew 8:29* - e. “What have we to do with you?” from Greek phrase <i>ti hēmin kai soi</i> is a Hebrew idiom signaling hostility or distance (Judges 11:12; 1 Kings 17:18).
*Matthew 8:34* - f. The townspeople's request for Jesus to leave may seem surprising after witnessing a miraculous deliverance, but it reflects the tension between divine power and human fear. In casting out the demons, Jesus disrupted their economy—the loss of the pigs likely represented a major financial blow. But more than material loss, his presence exposed spiritual realities they were not prepared to face. Like in Luke 5:8, where Peter falls at Jesus' feet and says, “Depart from me, for I am a sinful man,” this moment reveals how holiness can provoke discomfort, especially when it confronts hidden darkness. Rather than rejoice over the liberation of the men, the town recoils at the cost and the presence of one whose authority they could not control.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 9:1* - a. “hometown” (Matthew 4:13 identifies Capernaum as Jesus' “hometown”/<i>patris</i>; cf. 9:1).
*Matthew 9:2* - b. “stretcher” (Greek *phoreon* = portable bed/mattress; cf. 8:8, 9:6). “son” (Jesus uses *paidion*, often translated “child,” but context implies an adult male. “Son” retains affectionate tone).
*Matthew 9:6* - c. “bed” (Greek <i>kline</i> = household bed; replaced “stretcher” post-healing for readability).
*Matthew 9:13* - d. When Jesus quotes, “I desire mercy and not sacrifice,” He draws from Hosea 6:6 to confront the Pharisees' distorted understanding of righteousness. In its original context, this verse expresses YHWH's longing for covenantal loyalty—steadfast love expressed through compassionate action—rather than mere ritual compliance. Jesus applies it here to expose the emptiness of external religiosity that lacks mercy toward sinners. His mission is not to reward the self-righteous, but to call those aware of their need. The quotation reaffirms that God values a heart aligned with His mercy more than outward displays of piety, and it restores the prophetic call to love over legalism. The directive, “Go and learn what this means,” was a rabbinic challenge—an invitation to true understanding, not mere familiarity with Scripture.
*Matthew 9:15* - e. The “bridegroom” metaphor portrays Jesus as the long-awaited Christ and covenant redeemer, drawing on Old Testament imagery where God is depicted as Israel's husband (cf. Isaiah 54:5; Hosea 2:16–20). His presence marks a time of joy and celebration, not mourning. Just as wedding guests do not fast while the bridegroom is present, so Jesus' disciples rejoice in his presence. But the phrase “the bridegroom will be taken away” foreshadows his death—when sorrow and fasting will be appropriate. The metaphor emphasizes the newness, intimacy, and temporary nature of Jesus' earthly ministry.
*Matthew 9:16* - f. The patch metaphor emphasizes the futility of combining Jesus' new way with the old system of religious observance. An unshrunk patch sewn onto an old garment will pull away when washed, worsening the tear. Likewise, trying to attach Jesus' message of renewal to the brittle framework of legalism results in damage, not repair. The metaphor warns that the kingdom of God cannot be treated as a supplement to old covenant rituals—it demands a complete reweaving of the fabric of spiritual life.
*Matthew 9:17* - g. The image of new wine in old wineskins illustrates the incompatibility between Jesus' kingdom message and the old religious structures. New wine, still fermenting, would burst brittle old skins—just as the dynamic, Spirit-filled life of the kingdom cannot be contained by rigid traditions or legalistic systems. The “fresh wineskins” represent a renewed vessel—new hearts, new covenant, new community—capable of bearing the transformative power of Jesus' teaching without rupture. The metaphor affirms that Jesus did not come to patch up old religion, but to inaugurate something entirely new.
*Matthew 9:27* - h. The title “Son of David” is a messianic designation drawn from God's covenant with David in 2 Samuel 7:12–16, where He promises that David's throne will be established forever. By the time of Jesus, this phrase had become a widespread hope for a coming deliverer—a kingly figure who would restore Israel. When the blind men cry out, “Son of David,” they are not merely recognizing Jesus' lineage, but proclaiming Him as the long-awaited Messiah. Their plea reveals both theological insight and personal desperation: though physically blind, they perceive Jesus more clearly than many who see. This moment highlights how faith often resides not in physical sight, but in spiritual recognition of who Jesus truly is.
*Matthew 9:36* - i. “weary and scattered” (Greek <i>estenogrigoremēnoi kai errimmēnoi</i>; metaphor from Numbers 27:17; leadership crisis in Israel).
*Matthew 9:38* - j. The verb “pray” translates Greek <i>erōtan</i>, meaning “to ask” or “to petition,” often emphasizing earnest, specific requests (cf. Luke 11:9–13, where it underscores persistence in seeking divine provision). The metaphor of the “harvest” evokes Old Testament imagery (e.g., Joel 3:13; Revelation 14:14–20) of God’s eschatological gathering of people into His Kingdom, while “workers” highlights those commissioned to proclaim the Gospel and shepherd others. Jesus’ command reflects both the urgency of mission and reliance on divine initiative to raise laborers for spiritual harvest. A parallel request in Luke 10:2 precedes the sending of the seventy-two, linking prayer to active participation in God’s redemptive work (cf. also John 4:35–38).

**42** And whoever gives even a cup of cold water to one of these little ones because they are my disciple, truly I tell you, they will never lose their reward.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-10-42-l'>l</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 10:10* - a. “Provisions” refers to the basic needs of those doing God's work—such as food, shelter, and clothing. Jesus affirms that those who serve the kingdom should be supported by those they minister to.
*Matthew 10:14* - b. The gesture of shaking dust from one's feet was a symbolic act of renouncing responsibility and fellowship with that place. In Jewish custom, it was sometimes performed when leaving Gentile territory, expressing a break in spiritual solidarity. Here, it underscores the severity of rejecting the kingdom message. It marks that town as covenantally excluded.
*Matthew 10:15* - c. This comparative phrase expresses a severe divine judgment using the infamous fate of Sodom and Gomorrah as a benchmark. In context, it heightens the spiritual accountability of those who reject the message brought by Jesus' messengers. It also implies that judgment is graduated, not uniform. This is a key point in Jesus' theology of divine justice.
*Matthew 10:23* - d. This verse contains one of the most debated sayings of Jesus. The phrase “before the Son of Man comes” can be understood in several ways: as a reference to (1) Jesus' resurrection and post-resurrection appearances, (2) His coming in judgment against Jerusalem in 70 AD, or (3) His final return at the end of the age. The immediate context favors a near-term fulfillment—Jesus comforting His disciples during a time of Jewish mission and persecution. The phrase may reflect a recurring pattern in prophetic speech where one event (e.g., judgment on Israel) foreshadows a greater one (the final coming). Rather than specifying chronology, Jesus assures His disciples that their mission will not be in vain and that He will come in power before their work is exhausted.
*Matthew 10:25* - e. When Jesus says, “If they have called the master of the house Beelzebul,” he refers to accusations that he worked by demonic power (see Matthew 12:24). The term “Beelzebul” likely mocks Baal as “lord of dung.” His point is that disciples should not expect better treatment than their master. If Jesus himself was slandered, his followers will face even worse. The phrase “members of his household” emphasizes spiritual kinship—and shared suffering.
*Matthew 10:29* - f. Assarion (<i>assarion</i>) was a minor Roman bronze coin, roughly equivalent to 1/16 of a denarius, used for small transactions such as the sale of sparrows. The phrase “not one falls to the ground apart from your Father” (Greek: <i>aneu tou patros hymōn</i>) is open-ended and lacks an explicit verb like “wills” or “knows.” This leaves room for interpretive nuance: it could imply divine will, knowledge, presence, or permission. The ambiguity underscores the theological emphasis on God's intimate involvement in even the smallest events, without specifying the mode of that involvement.
*Matthew 10:30* - g. The statement “Even the hairs of your head are all numbered” uses hyperbole to express God's intimate care. In Jewish idiom, counting one's hairs meant absolute attentiveness—nothing escapes his knowledge. Jesus assures that even what seems trivial is known and valued by the Father.
*Matthew 10:34* - h. Jesus' statement is not a call to violence but a warning about the unavoidable division caused by the truth of the Gospel. The “sword” represents the piercing nature of God's Word and the Truth it reveals—dividing light from darkness, loyalty from rebellion, and even kin from kin. The Greek word <i>machaira</i> refers to a small sword or dagger, and in biblical usage, it often symbolizes judgment or spiritual separation (cf. Hebrews 4:12). Jesus is declaring that allegiance to Him will expose and fracture even the closest earthly relationships, just as prophesied in Micah 7:6. This sword is the dividing force of Truth—not violence, but conviction.
*Matthew 10:35* - i. The phrase “I have come to divide a man against his father…” echoes Micah 7:6, where the breakdown of family trust is described as a symptom of societal collapse. Jesus is not promoting conflict but revealing that allegiance to him often provokes division, even within the most sacred earthly bonds. Following him demands a higher loyalty than cultural or familial expectations. His coming exposes hearts and forces a choice—creating division not by aggression, but by truth.
*Matthew 10:39* - j. The phrase “Whoever finds their life will lose it…” expresses the paradox at the heart of discipleship. To “find your life” means to cling to ego—your self-centered identity, your control, your ambitions. But in grasping for self, you forfeit what matters most: life rooted in God. Conversely, “losing your life for my sake” is not merely about martyrdom—it's the daily surrender of ego, the laying down of personal sovereignty to receive divine purpose. In dying to the false self, you awaken to the true life God offers. Jesus invites us to trade the fragile illusion of control for the indestructible freedom of trust.
*Matthew 10:41* - k. The phrase “will receive a prophet's reward” refers to the shared blessing that comes from honoring a prophet's mission. In Jewish tradition, a prophet was God's mouthpiece, and to receive one—meaning to support, host, or heed their message—was to participate in their sacred work. Jesus affirms that even those who aren't prophets themselves but honor one because they recognize their role will share in their spiritual reward. The same applies to “a righteous person”—this speaks to how God notices not only those on the front lines, but also those who stand beside them in faith and solidarity.
*Matthew 10:42* - l. The phrase “a cup of cold water to one of these little ones” highlights how even the smallest act of kindness toward a disciple—motivated by their identity in Christ—is seen and rewarded by God. “Little ones” refers not to children, but to seemingly insignificant followers. Jesus affirms that no act of love done for his sake is ever overlooked.

#### Translation Notes

*Matthew 11:6* - b. The word translated as “turn away” comes from the Greek verb <i>skandalizō</i>, often rendered “stumble” or “fall away.” However, in modern English, “stumble” has become too soft, and “fall away” risks misinterpretation—as if Jesus were the cause of someone's sin or apostasy. In reality, <i>skandalizō</i> points to the offense or spiritual resistance people feel when confronted with Christ's humility, mercy, or unexpected ways. The rendering “turn away because of me” was chosen to preserve this tension: it describes the heart that recoils from Jesus when He does not meet worldly expectations. This becomes especially clear in context—John the Baptist, imprisoned and doubting, sends messengers to ask if Jesus is truly the One. Rather than rebuke, Jesus responds with blessing: the one who remains faithful, even when God's work unfolds in paradox, is blessed. This translation honors the original meaning while making it spiritually and emotionally accessible to the reader.
*Matthew 11:7* - c. The image of a “reed shaken by the wind” evokes instability, cowardice, or conformism—qualities opposite to John the Baptist's prophetic character. In first-century Palestine, reeds were common along the Jordan and easily bent by the breeze. Jesus uses the metaphor rhetorically to ask whether the people expected someone fickle or afraid. The implied answer is no: John was unwavering, even unto imprisonment. The phrase exposes the contrast between public fascination and actual recognition of prophetic truth. By raising this question, Jesus affirms John's moral fortitude and prophetic authority.
*Matthew 11:11* - d. Jesus declares John the Baptist to be the greatest of those “born of women”—a Hebrew idiom meaning all human beings. John's greatness lies in his role as the final prophet of the old covenant, the one who directly announced and identified the Christ. Yet paradoxically, the “least in the kingdom of heaven” is said to be greater—not in holiness or courage, but in spiritual position. John stood on the threshold of the new covenant but did not live to witness its fulfillment. Those who enter the kingdom after Christ's resurrection partake in a deeper spiritual reality—sealed by the Spirit, covered by the cross, and joined to the risen life of the Christ. It is a shift in redemptive history: greatness is no longer measured by proximity to prophecy, but by participation in the kingdom Christ has inaugurated.
*Matthew 11:12* - e. The Greek verbs <i>biazetai</i> (“is forcefully advancing”) and <i>harpazousin</i> (“are laying hold of it”) paint a picture of intensity and movement. The phrase “the zealous are laying hold of it” was chosen to express not just effort, but the emotional desperation of those who know they cannot afford to miss the kingdom. Jesus is not describing violent aggressors but spiritually awakened people—those whose longing for salvation pushes them past fear, pride, and delay. “Zealous” captures the soul-deep resolve of the poor in spirit, the broken, the hungry, who rush toward the kingdom as their only hope. This interpretation aligns with Luke 16:16, where Jesus says, “everyone is forcing their way into it”—not by violence, but by the urgency of awakened faith. Grammatically, <i>biazetai</i> may be understood either as a middle-voice verb (“the kingdom is forcefully advancing”) or as a passive (“is being attacked”), but in context, the middle fits best. The language points to the disruptive inbreaking of God's reign, met with passionate response by those who recognize its worth.
*Matthew 11:14* - f. When Jesus says, “he is Elijah,” he affirms that John the Baptist fulfills the long-awaited role prophesied in Malachi 4:5. The phrase “if you are willing to accept it” reveals that this identity is spiritually perceived rather than universally obvious. The crowd expected Elijah to return in fire, yet he came in camel's hair, calling for repentance. Jesus leaves room for mystery—John may not look like the Elijah of old, but he bears the mantle, the mission, and the spirit of the forerunner. This subtle ambiguity invites the listener to decide whether they are willing to see prophetic fulfillment not by appearance, but by function and divine appointment.
*Matthew 11:17* - g. The saying, “We played the flute for you, and you did not dance; we sang a dirge, and you did not mourn,” draws from a children's game mimicking weddings and funerals—occasions for celebration and sorrow. The metaphor rebukes the spiritual stubbornness of Jesus' generation: they refused to respond to either John's somber call to repentance or Jesus' joyful invitation to the kingdom. Like petulant children demanding a different tune, they judged the message based on the mood of the messenger. This reveals a deeper issue: it wasn't the form that offended them, but the truth itself.
*Matthew 11:19* - h. The accusation, “Look, a glutton and a drunkard, a friend of tax collectors and sinners,” reflects how critics distorted Jesus' table fellowship to discredit him. “Glutton” and “drunkard” were serious moral charges in Jewish culture (cf. Deuteronomy 21:20), and the phrase “friend of sinners” was used derisively to question his holiness. Yet Jesus embraces the scandal of grace—he did not avoid the unclean, but brought the kingdom to them. The final line, “wisdom is justified by her children,” means that the fruit of his ministry—transformed lives—proves the divine origin of his actions. Truth is not measured by public approval, but by its lasting result.
*Matthew 11:23* - i. Jesus rebukes Capernaum with language that echoes Isaiah 14:13–15, where the proud are cast down from heaven to Sheol. The word <i>Hades</i> translates the Greek <i>Hadēs</i>, referring to the realm of the dead—often associated with the Hebrew <i>Sheol</i>. It is not the final place of judgment (Gehenna), but a shadowed domain of spiritual exile and separation from the living. The contrast is sharp: though Capernaum had witnessed great miracles, it will not be exalted to divine glory, but will descend into death's domain. The warning signals both spiritual consequence and eschatological reversal—the proud will be brought low, and the unrepentant judged more severely than even Sodom.
*Matthew 11:25* - j. “Wise and intelligent” refers not to true godly wisdom but to those who consider themselves intellectually or spiritually competent—often the educated, self-assured, or religiously elite who depend on their own insight rather than divine revelation. This aligns with Isaiah 29:14 and 1 Corinthians 1:19, where God nullifies the wisdom of the wise and frustrates the discernment of the discerning, emphasizing that self-reliant wisdom stands opposed to God’s revelatory purposes. Paul expands this in 1 Corinthians 1:26–29, showing that God deliberately chooses what the world deems foolish to shame the wise, exalting humble dependence over intellectual pride. The word translated “praise” <i>exomologoumai soi</i> means to confess, thank, or openly acknowledge, not merely to express admiration. Here, it reflects Jesus’ profound agreement with the Father’s will in concealing truth from the self-sufficient and revealing it to the “infants”—those of childlike humility and openness to God’s revelation (cf. Matthew 18:3; Luke 10:21).
*Matthew 11:26* - k. Jesus affirms that the Father's choice to reveal divine truths to the humble, rather than to the self-assured, was not random, but rooted in mercy. The Greek word <i>eudokia</i> can mean “good pleasure” or “delight,” but in this context, it points beyond mere satisfaction to the deeper benevolence of God's plan. “Gracious will” captures this nuance: what pleases God is not only wise, but kind — a will shaped by grace. Jesus celebrates not just the hiddenness of the kingdom, but the generosity behind its unveiling. This phrasing makes explicit the Father's joy in lifting the lowly and revealing truth to those open enough to receive it.
*Matthew 11:30* - l. The traditional phrasing “Take my yoke upon you” (v. 29) draws on a rich but now largely unfamiliar metaphor. In the first-century Jewish context, a “yoke” (<i>zygos</i>) referred not only to the wooden harness that bound two oxen together for plowing — a symbol of labor, constraint, and submission — but also to a rabbi’s interpretive path, the ethical framework through which one lived out divine truth. This image carried profound spiritual weight, encompassing guidance, discipleship, relationship, empowerment, submission, partnership, liberation, comfort, fittingness, transformation, simplicity, grace, and peace. However, the physical yoke itself, though carefully shaped to fit the necks of the animals, still represented a binding and often burdensome reality. Even a well-carved yoke, intended to distribute the load evenly, still demanded effort and endurance, and an ill-fitting yoke could cause painful sores and strain, becoming a source of suffering. In the same way, a harsh or overly rigid religious system can crush the spirit and weary the soul, placing burdens too heavy to bear. By contrast, Jesus’ path is one of grace and freedom, not mere external obligation. The TTT rendering “Share my labor upon you” (v. 29) and “For my way is freeing and my burden is light” (v. 30) seek to capture this liberating spirit without the cultural distance that the yoke metaphor can create today. By choosing “freeing” instead of the traditional “easy” (from the Greek <i>chrēstos</i>, meaning “kind” or “well-fitting”), this rendering emphasizes the release from guilt, shame, and religious oppression, inviting the weary soul into a life marked by divine companionship, transformative purpose, and shared strength. This approach aims to preserve the full depth of the original teaching while making it more immediately accessible to modern readers.

**27** If I cast out demons by Beelzebul, by whom do your own people cast them out? They will be your judges.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-12-27-e'>e</a></sup>

**45** Then it goes and brings with it seven other spirits more evil than itself, and they settle there. The final state of that person becomes worse than the first. So will it be with this wicked generation also.”
### My True Family
**46** While he was still speaking to the crowds, his mother and brothers stood outside, wanting to speak with him.
**47** Someone told him, “Look, your mother and brothers are standing outside, wanting to speak with you.”
**48** But he replied, “Who is my mother, and who are my brothers?”
**49** Then he stretched out his hand toward his disciples and said, “Here are my mother and my brothers.
**50** For whoever does the will of my Father who is in heaven is my brother, and sister, and mother.”

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 12:4* - a. The phrase “bread of the Presence” refers to the 12 loaves that were set out continually before YHWH in the tabernacle and temple (see Leviticus 24:5–9). In Hebrew, it is called <i>lechem ha-panim</i>, literally “bread of the face” or “presence,” symbolizing covenant fellowship with God. Only priests were allowed to eat it under normal circumstances. By citing David's exception, Jesus highlights that human need can take precedence over ritual law—a principle grounded in the mercy of God rather than rigid legalism.
*Matthew 12:6* - b. In Second-Temple Judaism, the temple was the sacred dwelling place of God—central to worship, forgiveness, and identity. Jesus' statement that “something greater than the temple is here” would have shocked His hearers. He identifies Himself as the true meeting place between God and humanity, anticipating His role as the crucified and risen Son in whom God's presence fully dwells (cf. John 2:19–21; Colossians 2:9).
*Matthew 12:8* - c. When Jesus declares Himself “Lord of the Sabbath,” He asserts divine authority over one of the most sacred institutions in Jewish life. The Sabbath was instituted by YHWH at creation (Genesis 2:3) and codified in the Ten Commandments (Exodus 20:8–11). To claim lordship over it is to place Himself above Moses, above the Law, and even above time itself. This is not merely a statement of authority, but a revelation of His divine identity as the One who gave the Sabbath and now fulfills its deepest purpose—rest in God through Christ. The Greek phrase is <i>kyrios tou sabbatou</i>, which mirrors the authority title “Lord” used elsewhere for YHWH, intensifying the claim.
*Matthew 12:23* - d. The title “Son of David” was widely recognized as a messianic term rooted in 2 Samuel 7:12–16 and Isaiah 11:1. The crowd's question reflects their amazement and rising hope that Jesus might be the promised King who would restore Israel. However, the phrasing in Greek (<i>mēti houtos estin...?</i>) implies hesitation or doubt—it can be read as “Surely this can't be...?” indicating their internal conflict between astonishment and disbelief.
*Matthew 12:27* - e. Jesus refers to Jewish exorcists—perhaps disciples of the Pharisees or other traveling miracle workers—who were recognized as performing exorcisms. His argument exposes the inconsistency of accusing him of demonic collusion while approving their own. By saying “they will be your judges,” Jesus flips the accusation: their approval of other exorcists condemns their rejection of him.
## Chapter 13
### The Parable of the Farmer and the Soils
**1** That day, Jesus left the house and sat by the sea.
**2** Large crowds gathered around him, so he got into a boat and sat down, while the crowd stood on the shore.
**3** He spoke to them in parables: “A farmer went out to sow seed.”
**4** As he sowed, some seed fell along the path, and the birds came and devoured it.
**5** Other seed fell on rocky ground, where it sprang up quickly, having little soil.
**6** But when the sun rose, it was scorched, and since it had no roots, it withered.
**7** Other seed fell among thorns, and the thorns grew up and choked it.
**8** Yet other seed fell on good soil and produced a crop; some a hundredfold, some sixty, some thirty.
**9** Whoever has ears, let them listen.
### The Purpose of Parables and Their Explanation
**10** The disciples came to him and asked, “Why do you speak to them in parables?”
**11** He answered, “Because the mysteries of the kingdom of heaven have been given to you, but not to them.
**12** For those who have, more will be given, and they will have abundance. But those who do not have, even what they have will be taken away.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-12-a'>a</a></sup>
**13** This is why I speak to them in parables: Though hearing, they do not hear; seeing, they do not see or understand.
**14** In them the prophecy of Isaiah is fulfilled: ‘You will hear and never understand; you will see and never perceive.’
**15** For this people’s hearts have grown hard; they barely hear with their ears, and they have closed their eyes. Otherwise, they might see with their eyes, hear with their ears, understand with their hearts, and turn, and He would heal them.
**16** But blessed are your eyes, for they see, and your ears, for they hear.
**17** Truly I tell you, many prophets and righteous people longed to see what you see but did not see it, and to hear what you hear but did not hear it.
**18** Therefore, listen to the parable of the sower:
**19** When someone hears the word of the kingdom but does not understand it, the evil one comes and snatches away what was sown in their heart. This is the seed along the path.
**20** The seed on rocky ground is the one who hears the word and instantly receives it with joy,
**21** but since it has no root, it lasts only a short time. When trouble or persecution comes because of the word, they quickly fall away.
**22** The seed among thorns is the one who hears the word, but the worries of this age and the deceitfulness of wealth choke the word, making it unfruitful.
**23** But the seed on good soil is the one who hears the word and understands it, bearing fruit and yielding a hundredfold, sixty, or thirty.”
### The Parable of the Darnel
**24** He presented another parable to them: “The kingdom of heaven is like a man who sowed good seed in his field.
**25** But while his slaves were sleeping, his enemy came and sowed darnel among the wheat and went away.
**26** When the wheat sprouted and yielded grain, the darnel appeared too.
**27** The slaves came to the master of the house and said, ‘Master, did you not sow good seed in your field? Where then did the darnel come from?’
**28** He replied, ‘An enemy did this.’ The slaves asked, ‘Do you want us to gather them?’
**29** ‘No,’ he said, ‘lest you uproot the wheat with them.’
**30** Let both grow together until the harvest. Then I will tell the reapers, ‘First gather the darnel and tie it into bundles to burn it, then gather the wheat into my barn.’”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-30-b'>b</a></sup>
### The Mustard Seed and Yeast
**31** He presented another parable to them: “The kingdom of heaven is like a mustard seed that a man took and sowed in his field.
**32** It is the smallest of all the seeds, but when it grows, it surpasses the garden plants and becomes a tree, so that the birds of the air come and nest in its branches.”
**33** He told another parable: “The kingdom of heaven is like yeast that a woman took and put into three measures of flour until the whole batch was fully leavened.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-33-c'>c</a></sup>
### The Righteous Will Shine
**34** Jesus spoke all these things to the crowds in parables. He said nothing to them without a parable,
**35** to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet: ‘I will open my mouth in parables; I will declare things hidden since the foundation of the world.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-35-d'>d</a></sup>
**36** Then he left the crowds and went into the house. His disciples came to him and asked, “Explain to us the parable of the darnel in the field.”
**37** He answered, “The one who sows the good seed is the Son of Man.
**38** The field is the world, the good seed is the children of the kingdom, and the darnels are the children of the evil one.
**39** The enemy who sowed them is the devil. The harvest is the end of the age, and the reapers are angels.
**40** Just as the darnels are gathered and burned in the fire, so it will be at the end of the age.
**41** The Son of Man will send out his angels and they will gather from his kingdom all causes of sin and the workers of lawlessness,
**42** and throw them into the fiery furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-42-e'>e</a></sup>
**43** Then the righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom of their Father. Whoever has ears, let them listen.”
### The Hidden Pearl
**44** “The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field. When someone found it, they hid it again and, in their joy, sold all they had and bought that field.
**45** Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a merchant seeking fine pearls.”
**46** “When they found one of great value, they went away, sold all they had, and bought it.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-46-f'>f</a></sup>
**47** “Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a net let down into the sea, gathering fish of every kind.”
**48** When it was full, they pulled it onto the shore, sat down, and collected the good fish into baskets, but the bad they threw away.
**49** So it will be at the end of the age: the angels will separate the wicked from among the righteous.
**50** And they will throw them into the fiery furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.
**51** Jesus said to them, “Have you understood all these things?” They replied, “Yes.”
**52** He said to them, “Therefore, every <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribe" class="glossary-word">scribe</a> trained for the kingdom of heaven is like the master of a house who brings out of his storeroom both new and old things.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-52-g'>g</a></sup>
### Jesus Rejected at Nazareth
**53** When Jesus had finished these parables, he left that place.
**54** Coming to his hometown, he taught them in their synagogue, and they were astonished, saying, “Where did this man get this wisdom and these miracles?
**55** Is not this one the son of the carpenter? Is not his mother called Mary, and his brothers James, Joseph, Simon, and Judas?
**56** And are not all his sisters with us? Where then did this man get all these things?”
**57** And they were offended by him. But Jesus said to them, “A prophet is not without honor except in his hometown and in his own household.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-13-57-h'>h</a></sup>
**58** And he did not do many miracles there because of their unbelief.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 13:12* - a. The saying “For whoever has, more will be given… but whoever does not have, even what they have will be taken away” is a spiritual proverb Jesus repeats throughout the Gospels (Matthew 25:29; Mark 4:25; Luke 8:18). It illustrates a divine principle of revelation: those who respond with faith and receptivity are given greater understanding, while those who resist lose even the truth they once held. In context, Jesus applies this to the parables—His disciples “have” because they seek, ask, and follow, so they are entrusted with the mysteries of the kingdom (v. 11). The crowds, however, remain passive or hardened, and thus truth is veiled. The Greek construction highlights this contrast of spiritual responsiveness. The word “have” does not refer to possessions, but to an open posture toward God's word. This principle echoes Proverbs 9:9, “Give instruction to a wise man, and he will be still wiser,” and is directly connected to Jesus' repeated warnings to listen with intent (Mark 4:24; Luke 8:18).
*Matthew 13:30* - b. Farmers burned darnel (a poisonous wheat look-alike) outside the village to prevent reseeding. Jesus uses this known harvest practice to frame the final judgment.
*Matthew 13:33* - c. The Greek word for “yeast” is <i>zyme</i>, which in many Jewish contexts symbolized corruption or impurity (Exodus 12:15; Matthew 16:6). However, Jesus here reverses the symbol: the yeast represents the quiet, permeating power of the kingdom of heaven. Just as a small amount of leaven transforms a large batch of dough from within, so the kingdom spreads invisibly and irreversibly until it fills the whole. The image emphasizes hidden influence rather than external force.
*Matthew 13:35* - d. The word <i>zyme</i> refers to fermented dough used as a rising agent. In Scripture, leaven often symbolizes hidden influence—typically negative (such as sin or hypocrisy), but here it is used positively to illustrate the transforming power of the kingdom of heaven. The “3 measures of flour” would yield enough bread to feed more than 100 people, emphasizing the kingdom's overwhelming effect. Though it begins invisibly and in small form, its impact quietly permeates everything it touches.
*Matthew 13:42* - e. “Fiery furnace” alludes to Danielic imagery of divine judgment (Daniel 3:6, 3:23).
*Matthew 13:46* - f. The phrase “hid again” reflects common legal practice in the ancient world. According to 1st-century Jewish and Roman law, buried treasure did not automatically belong to the finder—it was tied to ownership of the land. By hiding the treasure again, the person ensures they can legally acquire it by purchasing the field. The action highlights urgent joy and wise intent: giving up everything for what is of immeasurable worth.
*Matthew 13:52* - g. The Greek word traditionally translated “scribe” <i>grammateus</i> referred to someone trained in the Torah—an expert in Jewish law. By saying “becomes a disciple,” Jesus implies that even these respected teachers must undergo transformation and submit to the new reality of the kingdom. The image of bringing out “new and old” treasures points to their role in weaving together the wisdom of the Hebrew Scriptures with the fresh revelation found in Christ. The kingdom does not discard the old—it fulfills it.
*Matthew 13:57* - h. The phrase “offended” translates the Greek <i>skandalizō</i>, from which we get the word “scandal.” It means to stumble over, to be repelled by, or to reject because of a perceived contradiction or unacceptability. The townspeople could not reconcile Jesus' divine wisdom and miracles with their familiarity with him. This paradox—divine glory clothed in local ordinariness—provoked contempt rather than faith.
## Chapter 14
### The Death of John the Baptist
**1** At that time, Herod the tetrarch heard the news about Jesus
**2** and said to his servants, “This is John the Baptist. He has risen from the dead, and that is why these miraculous powers are at work in him.”
**3** For Herod had arrested John, bound him, and put him in prison because of Herodias, his brother Philip's wife,
**4** because John had kept telling him, “It is not lawful for you to have her.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-14-4-a'>a</a></sup>
**5** Although he wanted to kill him, he feared the people, for they considered John a prophet.
**6** But on Herod's birthday, the daughter of Herodias danced before the company and pleased Herod,&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-14-6-b'>b</a></sup>
**7** so he promised with an oath to give her whatever she wished.
**8** At her mother's urging, she said, “Give me the head of John the Baptist here on a platter.”
**9** Although the king was distressed, because of his oaths and guests, he ordered it to be given.
**10** He sent and had John beheaded in prison.
**11** His head was brought on a platter, given to the girl, and she took it to her mother.
**12** John's disciples came, took the body, buried it, then went and reported to Jesus.
### Feeding the Five Thousand
**13** When Jesus heard this, he withdrew by boat to a solitary place, but the crowds, hearing of it, followed him on foot from the towns.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-14-13-c'>c</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 14:4* - a. John's rebuke comes from the Torah, specifically Leviticus 18:16 and 20:21, where taking a brother's wife is forbidden. Herod's act was seen as both adulterous and unlawful by Jewish standards.
*Matthew 14:6* - b. The daughter of Herodias is unnamed in the Gospels, but known from Josephus as Salome. Her dance, likely suggestive in nature, exemplifies the decadence of Herod's court.
*Matthew 14:13* - c. The Greek verb <i>anachōreō</i> means to withdraw, often for solitude or prayer. Jesus likely sought quiet after hearing of John's death.
*Matthew 14:14* - d. “Was moved with compassion” translates the verb <i>esplagchnisthē</i>, expressing deep, gut-level divine mercy—not mere pity.
*Matthew 14:19* - e. Jesus' actions—blessing, breaking, giving—foreshadow the Last Supper. The parallel verbs link daily provision with sacrificial meaning.
*Matthew 14:21* - f. The word “men” is from the Greek <i>andres</i>, meaning adult males. The crowd total, including women and children, likely exceeded 10,000.
*Matthew 14:22* - g. The Greek verb <i>ēnankasen</i> means 'to compel' or 'to strongly urge.' “Urgently” matches the forcefulness of the moment better than “immediately”.
*Matthew 14:25* - h. “Fourth watch” refers to 3–6 a.m., the last Roman division of the night—emphasizing exhaustion and divine timing.
*Matthew 14:27* - i. “It is I” translates <i>egō eimi</i>, which can bear divine overtones. Here, it provides assurance rather than a theophanic claim.
*Matthew 14:30* - j. The verb <i>katapontizesthai</i> means to sink deeply—used here to portray Peter's physical and spiritual faltering.
*Matthew 14:36* - k. “Fringe of his garment” refers to the <i>tzitzit</i>, or tassels commanded in Numbers 15:38. The Greek verb <i>diasōthēsan</i> means to be delivered, healed, or made whole.

**1** Then the <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a> and Pharisees from Jerusalem came to Jesus, saying:

**7** Hypocrites! Isaiah rightly prophesied about you, saying:
**8** ‘This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me.
**9** In vain do they worship me, teaching as doctrines the commandments of men.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-15-9-b'>b</a></sup>
### What Defiles a Person
**10** He called the crowd and said, “Listen and understand:
**11** It is not what goes into the mouth that defiles a person; but what comes out of the mouth;this defiles a person.”
**12** Then his disciples came and said, “Do you know the Pharisees were offended by this statement?”
**13** He answered, “Every plant my Father has not planted will be uprooted.”
**14** “Leave them alone. They are blind guides leading the blind, and if the blind lead the blind, both will fall into a pit.”

**17** “Do you not see that whatever enters the mouth goes into the stomach and is expelled?”

### The Faith of a Canaanite Woman
**21** Jesus left there and went to the region of Tyre and Sidon.
**22** A Canaanite woman from that region came out, crying, “Lord, Son of David, have mercy on me! My daughter is severely tormented by a demon.”
**23** But he did not answer her. His disciples urged him, “Send her away, for she keeps crying out after us.”
**24** He replied, “I was sent only to the lost sheep of the house of Israel.”
**25** But she came and knelt before him, saying, “Lord, help me!”
**26** He replied, “It is not fitting to take the children's bread and throw it to the dogs.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-15-26-c'>c</a></sup>
**27** “Yes, Lord,” she said, “but even the dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their masters' table.”
**28** Then Jesus said, “Dear woman, great is your faith! Let it be done for you as you desire.” And her daughter was healed from that very hour.
### Healing the Multitudes
**29** Jesus walked along the Sea of Galilee, went up the mountain, and sat down.
**30** Great crowds came to him, bringing the lame, the blind, the crippled, the mute, and many others. They laid them at his feet, and he healed them.
**31** The crowd marveled as they saw the mute speaking, the crippled made whole, the lame walking, and the blind seeing. And they praised the God of Israel.
### Feeding the Four Thousand
**32** Then Jesus called his disciples and said, “I have compassion for the crowd, because they have already been with me three days and have nothing to eat. I do not want to send them away hungry, lest they collapse on the way.”
**33** His disciples said, “Where can we get enough bread in this desolate place to feed such a crowd?”
**34** Jesus asked, “How many loaves do you have?” They replied, “Seven, and a few small fish.”
**35** He instructed the crowd to sit on the ground.
**36** Taking the seven loaves and the fish, he gave thanks, broke them, and gave them to the disciples, who shared them with the crowd.
**37** They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples collected seven full baskets of leftovers.
**38** Those who ate were four thousand men, besides women and children.
**39** After sending the crowd away, he got into the boat and went to the region of Magadan.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 15:6* - a. Jesus rebukes them for using human traditions—like the “Corban” vow—to nullify God's clear command to honor and care for one's parents. By declaring resources “devoted to God,” they avoided supporting their parents while still appearing righteous. His charge exposes how pious-sounding customs were weaponized to override divine law.
*Matthew 15:9* - b. “Commandments of men” refers to teachings created by altering God's actual commandments—either by changing the words or redefining their meaning. These man-made rules were treated as divine truth, but Jesus declares such worship empty.
*Matthew 15:26* - c. The phrase “household dogs” translates the Greek word <i>kynaria</i>, a diminutive form of <i>kyōn</i> (“dog”), which refers to small, domestic animals kept in the home—not wild scavengers. In Jewish culture, “dogs” often symbolized Gentiles as outsiders to the covenant. Jesus' use of the softened term reflects a testing tone rather than an insult. By framing Gentile access as indirect (“crumbs”), He both affirms Israel's covenant priority and opens space for persistent faith to be honored. Her response reveals spiritual insight and humility, and Jesus' approval marks her as a forerunner of Gentile inclusion in the kingdom.

**19** I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven; whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-16-19-d'>d</a></sup>

### The Cost of Discipleship
**24** Then Jesus said to his disciples, “If anyone desires to come after me, they must deny themselves, take up their cross, and follow me.”

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 16:4* - a. The term 'adulterous generation' refers to a spiritually unfaithful people, echoing the language of the Old Testament prophets like Hosea, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel, who described Israel's idolatry and covenant betrayal as spiritual adultery. In this context, Jesus is condemning the generation that rejects his message despite the clear evidence of his divine authority. The 'sign of Jonah' refers to Jonah's three days in the belly of the great fish (Jonah 1:17), foreshadowing Jesus' own death, burial, and resurrection. It is the only sign Jesus offers as proof to those demanding miraculous confirmation, emphasizing faith in his resurrection over spectacle.
*Matthew 16:12* - b. ‘Leaven’ was a common Jewish metaphor for influence or corruption. Jesus used it here to refer not to physical bread but to the doctrinal distortion and spiritual hypocrisy of the Pharisees and Sadducees.
*Matthew 16:18* - c. Jesus' statement, 'You are Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church,' includes a deliberate Greek wordplay between 'Petros' ('stone') and 'petra' ('rock' or 'bedrock').
*Matthew 16:19* - d. The 'keys of the kingdom of heaven' represent spiritual authority to open and shut access, drawn from Jewish imagery (cf. Isaiah 22:22).
*Matthew 16:23* - e. The phrase 'merely human concerns' refers to thoughts shaped by natural instinct, fear, or worldly reasoning.
*Matthew 16:25* - f. The Greek word for 'life' is 'psychē,' which also means 'soul,' referring to one's eternal self.
*Matthew 16:26* - g. The phrase 'forfeit their soul' uses the Greek verb 'zēmiōthēnai,' meaning to suffer loss, be penalized, or forfeit.
*Matthew 16:28* - h. This verse has several interpretations, including the Transfiguration, the resurrection, Pentecost, or the 70 AD judgment on Jerusalem.
## Chapter 17
### The Transfiguration
**1** After six days, Jesus took Peter, James, and his brother John up a high mountain alone.
**2** He was transfigured before them; his face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as bright as light.
**3** And behold, Moses and Elijah appeared, talking with him.
**4** Peter said to Jesus, “Lord, it is good for us to be here; if you wish, let us set up three shelters: one for you, one for Moses, and one for Elijah.”
**5** While he was still speaking, behold, a bright cloud overshadowed them, and a voice from the cloud said, “This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased. Listen to him!”
**6** When the disciples heard this, they fell facedown to the ground, terrified.
**7** But Jesus came, touched them, and said, “Rise, do not be afraid.”
**8** When they lifted up their eyes, they saw no one but Jesus alone.
### Elijah Has Come Already
**9** As they were coming down the mountain, Jesus commanded them, “Tell no one about the vision until the Son of Man is risen from the dead.”
**10** His disciples asked him, “Why then do the <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a> say that Elijah must come first?”
**11** Jesus replied, “Indeed, Elijah comes first and will restore all things.”
**12** “But I tell you, Elijah has already come, and they did not recognize him but did to him however they wished. In the same way, the Son of Man will also suffer at their hands.”
**13** Then the disciples understood that he was speaking to them about John the Baptist.
### Healing a Demon-Possessed Boy
**14** When they came to the crowd, a man approached Jesus, knelt before him, and said,
**15** “Lord, have mercy on my son, for he is an epileptic, suffers terribly, and often falls into fire or water.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-17-15-b'>b</a></sup>
**16** “I brought him to your disciples, but they could not heal him.”
**17** Jesus said, “You faithless and twisted generation, how long must I be with you? How long must I bear with you? Bring him here to me.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-17-17-a'>a</a></sup>
**18** Jesus rebuked the demon, and it came out of him, and the boy was healed from that very hour.
**19** Then the disciples came to Jesus privately and asked, “Why could we not cast it out?”
**20** Because of your little faith. Truly I tell you, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, ‘Move from here to there,’ and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you.
**21** [“But this kind only comes out by prayer and fasting.”]
### The Coming Resurrection
**22** While they were staying in Galilee, Jesus said to them, “The Son of Man is about to be betrayed into the hands of men,”
**23** “and they will kill him, and on the third day he will be raised up.” And they were deeply grieved.
### The Temple Tax
**24** When they came to Capernaum, the collectors of the temple tax approached Peter and asked, “Does your teacher not pay the temple tax?”
**25** He replied, “Yes.” When he entered the house, Jesus anticipated him, saying, “What do you think, Simon? From whom do the kings of the earth collect taxes or customs, from their own sons or from outsiders?”
**26** Peter answered, “From outsiders.” Jesus said to him, “Then the sons are free.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-17-26-c'>c</a></sup>
**27** “But so we do not offend them, go to the sea, cast a hook, and take the first fish you catch. When you open its mouth, you will find a four-drachma coin. Take it and give it to them for me and you.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-17-27-d'>d</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 17:15* - b. In ancient Jewish and Greco-Roman contexts, epilepsy was often attributed to lunar influence—hence the Greek word <i>selēniazetai</i> (“moonstruck”). The mention of “fire and water” reflects common household dangers of the time: open flames for cooking and open cisterns or wells for water. The boy's seizures placed him at risk of falling into these hazards, reinforcing both the physical and spiritual urgency of his condition.
*Matthew 17:17* - a. Jesus' lament echoes the prophetic language of Deuteronomy 32:5, where Israel is called a “twisted generation” for turning away from God despite witnessing His works. The Greek words translated “faithless and twisted” express not just doubt, but a refusal to trust and a distortion of heart and mind away from truth. Though the rebuke applies broadly to the surrounding generation, it also includes the disciples, who had just failed to cast out the demon due to their lack of faith (v.20). Jesus speaks with sorrowful urgency, not condemnation—grieved by a people who have seen much, yet still resist belief. His frustration reflects the burden of the Messiah who walks among a spiritually misaligned people, longing to restore their faith.
*Matthew 17:26* - c. Jesus' question teaches that, just as royal children are exempt from taxes levied by their father, the Son of God is not obligated to pay temple tax to His own Father. His point is that the temple ultimately belongs to God—and therefore to Him. Yet, for the sake of peace and to avoid offense, He chooses to pay.
*Matthew 17:27* - d. The “four-drachma coin” refers to a silver coin called a <i>statēr</i>, worth 4 drachmas—the exact amount needed to pay the temple tax for 2 people (2 drachmas each). This value corresponds to 1 shekel, as described in Exodus 30:13, where every male was required to pay a half-shekel as atonement money. Although the Old Testament uses the term “shekel,” Greek-speaking Jews in the 1st century often paid the tax using drachma-based currency minted in Tyre or elsewhere.
## Chapter 18
### The Greatest in the Kingdom
**1** At that time the disciples came to Jesus and asked, “Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?”

**7** Woe to the world for the temptations it causes, for such things are inevitable, but doomed is the person through whom they come!&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-18-7-a'>a</a></sup>
**8** If your hand or foot causes you to stumble, cut it off and throw it away. It is better to enter life maimed or crippled than to have two hands or two feet and be thrown into the eternal fire.
**9** And if your eye causes you to stumble, gouge it out and throw it away. It is better to enter life with one eye than to have two eyes and be thrown into the fire of <a href="glossary.xhtml#gehenna" class="glossary-word">Gehenna</a>.
### The Parable of the Lost Sheep
**10** See that you do not despise one of these little ones, for I tell you, their angels in heaven always see the face of my Father in heaven.
**11** For the Son of Man has come to save the lost.
**12** What do you think? If a man has a hundred sheep and one of them goes astray, will he not leave the ninety‑nine on the hills and go to find the one that went astray?
**13** And if he finds it, truly I tell you, he rejoices more over that one than over the ninety‑nine that did not stray.
**14** In the same way, it is not the will of your Father in heaven that any of these little ones should perish.
### Confronting a Dear One in Sin
**15** If a dear one sins against you, go and point out their fault between you and them alone. If they listen to you, you have won them over.
**16** But if they will not listen, take one or two others along, so that every accusation may be confirmed by the testimony of two or three witnesses.
**17** If they refuse to listen to them, share it with the church. And if they refuse to listen even to the church, treat them as you would a <a href="glossary.xhtml#gentile" class="glossary-word">Gentile</a> or a tax collector.
**18** Truly I tell you, whatever you restrain on earth will be restrained in heaven, and whatever you set free on earth will be set free in heaven.
**19** Again, truly I tell you, if two of you agree on earth about anything they ask, it will be done for them by my Father in heaven.
**20** For where two or three gather in my name, there I am in their midst.
### The Parable of the Unforgiving Servant
**21** Then Peter came to him and asked, “Lord, how many times should I forgive a dear one if they sin against me? Up to seven times?”
**22** Jesus said to him, “I do not say up to seven times, but up to seventy times seven.”
**23** Therefore, the kingdom of heaven is like a king who wanted to settle accounts with his servants.
**24** As he began to settle, a servant who owed him ten thousand talents was brought to him.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-18-24-b'>b</a></sup>
**25** Since he could not pay, his master ordered that he, his wife, his children, and all he had be sold to repay the debt.
**26** The servant fell down before him and said, ‘Master, have patience with me, and I will repay you everything.’
**27** The master of that servant took pity on him, released him, and forgave the debt.
**28** But that servant went out and found one of his fellow servants who owed him a hundred denarii. He seized him and began to choke him, saying, ‘Pay me what you owe!’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-18-28-c'>c</a></sup>
**29** His fellow servant fell at his feet and begged him, ‘Have patience with me, and I will repay you.’
**30** But he refused and threw him into prison until he could pay back what he owed.
**31** When his fellow servants saw what happened, they were deeply grieved and went to their master and reported all that had happened.
**32** Then his master called him and said, ‘You wicked servant! I forgave all that debt because you begged me.’
**33** Should you not have had mercy on your fellow servant, just as I had mercy on you?
**34** In his anger, his master handed him over to be tortured until he paid all he owed.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-18-34-d'>d</a></sup>
**35** So also my Father in heaven will do to you if each of you does not forgive your brothers and sisters from the heart.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 18:7* - a. The Greek term <i>ouai</i> is commonly rendered as “woe” in most English translations, but this term can be archaic and unclear in its precise intention on first read. In this verse, it captures both divine sorrow and severe judgment. The first occurrence was rendered as “grief” to emphasize the layered compassion and lament Jesus felt for a world that inevitably produces temptations. The second occurrence was rendered as “doomed” to reflect the certainty of divine judgment upon those who cause others to stumble, as described in verse 6, where such a person is said to be better off drowned with a millstone around their neck, emphasizing the irreversible nature of their condemnation.
*Matthew 18:24* - b. The Greek term for “talent” (<i>talanton</i>) refers to the largest unit of currency in the ancient world, often representing about 6,000 denarii. In this parable, ten thousand talents would equate to approximately 60 million days' wages, an impossibly large debt intended to symbolize the vast, unpayable nature of human sin before God. The exaggeration highlights the incomprehensible grace and forgiveness of the king, representing God.
*Matthew 18:28* - c. A denarius (<i>denarion</i>) was the typical daily wage for a laborer in Roman times. One hundred denarii, therefore, represents roughly 100 days' wages—substantial but manageable compared to the astronomical debt of ten thousand talents. The contrast emphasizes the servant's unwillingness to extend even a small portion of the forgiveness he received.
*Matthew 18:34* - d. The Greek word used here, <i>basanistais</i>, implies more than just imprisonment; it indicates being handed over to tormentors or torturers. This severe consequence highlights the gravity of the servant's unforgiveness, emphasizing that divine judgment on those who refuse to forgive is not merely punitive but involves suffering proportionate to the offense.

**7** They said, “Why then did Moses command to give a certificate of divorce and send her away?”
**8** He answered, “Moses permitted you to divorce your wives because of the hardness of your hearts, but it was not so from the beginning.”
**9** I tell you, any man who divorces his wife, except for sexual covenant treachery, and marries another, commits adultery. And any man who marries a divorced woman commits adultery.
### Welcoming the Little Children
**10** His disciples said to him, “If this is the case between a man and his wife, it is better not to marry.”
**11** Jesus replied, “Not everyone can accept this word, but only those to whom it has been given.
**12** For there are eunuchs who were born this way, and there are eunuchs made so by others, and there are eunuchs who made themselves so for the sake of the kingdom of heaven. Let the one who can accept this teaching receive it.”
**13** Then little children were brought to him that he might lay his hands on them and pray, but the disciples rebuked them.
**14** But Jesus said, “Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of heaven belongs to such as these.”
**15** And he laid his hands on them and departed from there.
### The Rich Young Man
**16** Then someone came to him and asked, “Good Teacher, what good thing must I do to have eternal life?”
**17** So he said to him, “Why do you call me good? No one is good but One, that is, God. But if you want to enter into the life of God, keep the commandments.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-19-17-a'>a</a></sup>
**18** He asked, “Which ones?” Jesus replied, “‘You shall not murder,’ ‘You shall not commit adultery,’ ‘You shall not steal,’ ‘You shall not bear false witness,’
**19** ‘Honor your father and mother,’ and, ‘You shall love your neighbor as yourself.’”
**20** The young man said to him, “All these I have kept from my youth. What do I still lack?”
**21** Jesus said to him, “If you want to be perfect, go, sell your possessions and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me.”
**22** When the young man heard this, he went away sorrowful, for he had many possessions.
### The Danger of Wealth
**23** Then Jesus said to his disciples, “Truly, I tell you, it is hard for a rich person to enter the kingdom of heaven.
**24** Again, I tell you, it is easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God.”
**25** When the disciples heard this, they were profoundly dismayed and said, “Who then can be saved?”
**26** But Jesus looked at them and said, “With people this is impossible, but with God all things are possible.”
### The Reward of Sacrifice
**27** Then Peter answered him, “Look, we have left everything and followed you. What then will we have?”
**28** Jesus said to them, “Truly, I tell you, at the renewal of all things, when the Son of Man sits on his glorious throne, you who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel.
**29** And everyone who has left houses, brothers, sisters, father, mother, children, or lands for my name's sake will receive a hundredfold and will inherit eternal life.
**30** But many who are first will be last, and the last first.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-19-30-b'>b</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 19:17* - a. The phrase "enter into the life of God" reflects the deeper theological concept behind the Greek term <i>zōē aiōnios</i>, which signifies not merely endless existence but a profound participation in the divine nature, as emphasized in passages like 2 Peter 1:4 and John 17:3. This understanding aligns with the broader biblical vision of eternal life as living in intimate relationship with God, beyond mere duration.
*Matthew 19:30* - b. The phrase “many who are first will be last, and the last first” captures a recurring theme in Jesus' teachings about the reversal of worldly status in the kingdom of God. This principle emphasizes that earthly measures of greatness, privilege, and rank often hold little value in God's economy, where humility, service, and self-sacrifice are exalted. It echoes themes from the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12) and the parable of the workers in the vineyard (Matthew 20:1-16), illustrating that divine reward is based on God's grace rather than human merit or status. The underlying Greek phrase, <i>eschaton esontai prōtoi</i>, reflects a radical inversion of human expectations.
## Chapter 20
### The Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard
**1** “The kingdom of heaven is like a landowner who went out early in the morning to hire workers for his vineyard.”
**2** “He agreed with the workers for one <a href="glossary.xhtml#denarius" class="glossary-word">denarius</a> for the day and sent them into his vineyard.”
**3** “About the third hour he went out, saw others standing idle in the marketplace,”
**4** and said, ‘You also go into the vineyard, and I will pay you what is just.’ So they went.
**5** He went out again about the sixth and the ninth hour and did the same.
**6** About the eleventh hour he found others standing and said, ‘Why are you standing here all day doing nothing?’
**7** They answered, ‘Because no one has hired us.’ He said, ‘You also go into the vineyard, and you will receive what is just.’
**8** When evening came, the owner of the vineyard told his foreman, ‘Call the workers and pay them their wages, beginning with the last and ending with the first.’
**9** The ones hired at the eleventh hour came and each received a <a href="glossary.xhtml#denarius" class="glossary-word">denarius</a>.
**10** When the first came, they expected more, yet they also received a <a href="glossary.xhtml#denarius" class="glossary-word">denarius</a>.
**11** On receiving it, they grumbled against the landowner,
**12** saying, ‘These last worked only one hour, and you have made them equal to us who bore the burden of the day and the scorching heat.’
**13** But he answered one of them, ‘Friend, I am not wronging you. Did you not agree with me for a <a href="glossary.xhtml#denarius" class="glossary-word">denarius</a>?’
**14** ‘Take what is yours and go. I choose to give to this last as I gave to you.’
**15** ‘Am I not free to do what I want with my own money? Or are you envious because I am generous?’
**16** Thus the last will be first and the first last. For many are called, but few are chosen.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-20-16-a'>a</a></sup>
### Jesus Predicts His Death a Third Time
**17** As Jesus was going up to Jerusalem, he took the twelve aside on the road and said,
**18** “Take heart, we are going up to Jerusalem, and the Son of Man will be delivered to the chief priests and <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a>; they will condemn him to death”
**19** “and hand him over to the <a href="glossary.xhtml#gentiles" class="glossary-word">Gentiles</a> to mock, flog, and crucify him; and on the third day he will rise.”
### A Mother's Request
**20** Then the mother of Zebedee's sons came to him with her sons, kneeling and asking a favor of him.
**21** He said to her, “What do you want?” She said, “Say that these two sons of mine may sit, one at your right hand and one at your left, in your kingdom.”
**22** Jesus replied, “You do not know what you are asking. Are you able to drink the cup I am about to drink?” They said to him, “We are able.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-20-22-b'>b</a></sup>
**23** He said to them, “You will indeed drink my cup, but to sit at my right and left is not mine to grant; it is for those for whom my Father has prepared it.”
**24** When the ten heard, they were indignant with the two brothers.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-20-24-c'>c</a></sup>
**25** But Jesus called them and said, “You know that the rulers of the <a href="glossary.xhtml#gentiles" class="glossary-word">Gentiles</a> lord it over them, and the great exercise authority over them.”
**26** “It shall not be so among you. Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant,”
**27** “and whoever wants to be first among you must be your slave,”
**28** “just as the Son of Man did not come to be served but to serve and to give his life as a ransom for many.”
### Jesus Heals Two Blind Men
**29** As they were leaving Jericho, a great crowd followed him.
**30** Two blind men sitting by the road, hearing that Jesus was passing, cried out, “Have mercy on us, Lord, Son of David!”
**31** The crowd rebuked them to be silent, but they cried out all the louder, “Have mercy on us, Lord, Son of David!”
**32** Jesus stopped, called them, and said, “What do you want me to do for you?”
**33** They said to him, “Lord, that our eyes may be opened.”
**34** Moved with compassion, Jesus touched their eyes; immediately they regained sight and followed him.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 20:16* - a. This closing statement, “Therefore, the last will be first, and the first last,” reflects a recurring theme in Jesus' teaching about the reversal of worldly status in the kingdom of heaven. It emphasizes that divine reward is based not on the duration or apparent prominence of one's work but on the grace and generosity of God. This parable challenges human notions of fairness, illustrating that God's ways are not bound by human calculations but reflect a radically generous and merciful order (cf. Matthew 19:30, Luke 13:30).
*Matthew 20:22* - b. The phrase “baptism I am about to undergo (by blood)” refers to the profound suffering and sacrificial death Jesus was soon to face. In this context, “baptism” symbolizes the complete immersion into suffering and death, a common metaphor in both Jewish and early Christian thought (cf. Luke 12:50, Mark 10:38-39). This “baptism” highlights the totality of his sacrifice, not just physical suffering but the full weight of spiritual and redemptive purpose.
*Matthew 20:24* - c. The term “indignant” in this verse captures a deeper sense of moral outrage among the ten disciples. The Greek term <i>aganakteō</i> (ἀγανακτέω) conveys a strong, almost righteous anger at perceived unfairness or presumption. This response likely arose from a sense of betrayal or favoritism, as the request for special status directly contradicted the servant-hearted values Jesus had repeatedly emphasized (cf. Matthew 18:1-4, Mark 10:41).
## Chapter 21
### The Triumphal Entry
**1** As they approached Jerusalem and came to Bethphage at the Mount of Olives, Jesus sent two disciples,
**2** He told them, “Go into the village ahead of you, and right there you will find a donkey tied, and a colt with her. Untie them and bring them to me.
**3** If anyone asks you, ‘Why are you doing this?’ say, ‘The Lord needs them,’ and he will send them right away.”
**4** This happened to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet:
**5** “Say to the daughter of Zion: ‘Look, your King is coming to you, humble and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey.’”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-21-5-a'>a</a></sup>
**6** So the disciples went and did as Jesus instructed them.
**7** They brought the donkey and the colt, placed their cloaks on them, and Jesus sat on them.
**8** A large crowd spread their cloaks on the road, while others cut branches from the trees and spread them on the path.
**9** The crowds that went ahead and those that followed shouted: “Hosanna to the Son of David! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord! Hosanna in the highest!”
**10** When Jesus entered Jerusalem, the whole city was stirred and asked, “Who is this?”
**11** The crowds replied, “This is Jesus, the prophet from Nazareth in Galilee.”
### Cleansing the Temple
**12** Jesus entered the temple and drove out all who were buying and selling. He overturned the tables of the money changers and the benches of those selling doves.
**13** He said to them, “It is written, ‘My house will be called a house of prayer,’ but you have made it a den of thieves.”
**14** Then the blind and the lame came to him at the temple, and he healed them.
**15** But when the chief priests and <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a> saw the wonderful things he did, and the children shouting in the temple, “Hosanna to the Son of David!”, they were indignant
**16** They said to him, “Do you hear what these children are saying?” Jesus replied, “Yes. Have you never read, ‘Out of the mouths of children and infants you have perfected praise’?”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-21-16-b'>b</a></sup>
**17** Then he left them and went out of the city to Bethany, where he spent the night.
### The Withered Fig Tree
**18** In the morning, as he returned to the city, he became hungry.
**19** Seeing a fig tree by the road, he went to it but found nothing except leaves. He said to it, “May you never bear fruit ever again.” And at once, the fig tree withered.
**20** When the disciples saw this, they were astonished and said, “How did the fig tree wither so quickly?”
**21** Jesus answered, “Truly, I tell you, if you have faith and do not doubt, not only will you do what was done to the fig tree, but even if you say to this mountain, ‘Be lifted up and thrown into the sea,’ it will happen.
**22** And anything you ask in prayer, believing, you will receive.”
### The Authority of Jesus Questioned
**23** When Jesus entered the temple, the chief priests and the elders confronted him as he was teaching and said, “By what authority are you doing these things, and who gave you this authority?”
**24** Jesus replied, “I will also ask you one question, and if you answer me, I will tell you by what authority I do these things.
**25** The baptism of John; where was it from? From heaven or from people?” They discussed this among themselves, saying, “If we say, ‘From heaven,’ he will ask, ‘Then why did you not believe him?’
**26** But if we say, ‘From people,’ we fear the crowd, for they all regard John as a prophet.”
**27** So they answered Jesus, “We do not know.” Then he said, “Neither will I tell you by what authority I do these things.”
### The Parable of the Two Sons
**28** “What do you think? A man had two sons. He went to the first and said, ‘Son, go and work today in my vineyard.’
**29** The son answered, ‘I will not,’ but later he regretted it and went.
**30** Then the man went to the second son and said the same. He answered, ‘I will, sir,’ but he did not go.
**31** Which of the two did the will of his father?” They replied, “The first.” Jesus said, “Truly, I tell you, tax collectors and prostitutes are entering the kingdom of God ahead of you.
**32** For John came to you in the way of <a href="glossary.xhtml#righteousness" class="glossary-word">righteousness</a>, but you did not believe him. Yet tax collectors and prostitutes believed him, and even after you saw this, you did not repent and believe him.”
### The Parable of the Wicked Tenants
**33** Listen to another parable: There was a landowner who planted a vineyard, put a hedge around it, dug a winepress, and built a tower. Then he leased it to tenants and went away.
**34** When the harvest approached, he sent his servants to the tenants to collect his fruit.
**35** But the tenants seized his servants. They beat one, killed another, and stoned a third.
**36** Again, he sent more servants than the first, and they treated them the same way.
**37** Then last of all he sent his son to them, saying, ‘They will respect my son.’
**38** But when the tenants saw the son, they said to each other, ‘This is the heir. Come, let us kill him and take his inheritance.’
**39** So they seized him, threw him out of the vineyard, and killed him.
**40** So when the owner of the vineyard comes, what will he do to those tenants?
**41** They replied, “He will destroy those wicked tenants in a terrible way and lease the vineyard to others who will give him the fruits in their season.”
**42** Jesus said to them, “Have you never read in the Scriptures: ‘The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone. This was the Lord's doing, and it is marvelous in our eyes.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-21-42-c'>c</a></sup>
**43** Therefore, I tell you, the kingdom of God will be taken from you and given to a people who will produce its fruit.
**44** And whoever falls on this stone will be broken, but on whomever it falls, it will crush them to pieces.
**45** When the chief priests and Pharisees heard his parables, they realized he was speaking about them.
**46** But when they sought to arrest him, they feared the crowds, because they considered him to be a prophet.”

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 21:5* - a. The phrase “the foal of a donkey” is a reference to a young, unbroken donkey, emphasizing the humility and peace of the Messiah's approach, in contrast to the war horses typically ridden by conquering kings. This echoes the prophecy of Zechariah 9:9, symbolizing a peaceful, righteous ruler rather than a military conqueror.
*Matthew 21:16* - b. The phrase “you have perfected praise” is a direct quotation from Psalm 8:2 in the Greek Septuagint, where the word <i>katartizō</i> (perfected) carries the sense of establishing, completing, or making fully ready. It emphasizes the idea that even the simplest, most unpolished voices can offer pure and complete praise, reflecting God's ability to bring divine purpose to full expression through humble vessels.
*Matthew 21:42* - c. The phrase “the stone the builders rejected” is a direct reference to Psalm 118:22-23, which originally described the surprising victory of a seemingly insignificant stone becoming the most critical part of the structure. In the context of this parable, Jesus identifies himself as the cornerstone, rejected by the religious leaders but chosen by God to be the foundation of the new covenant. The term “marvelous” captures both the unexpected nature and the divine significance of this reversal, aligning with the broader biblical theme of God's wisdom confounding human expectations.
## Chapter 22
### The Parable of the Wedding Feast
**1** Jesus spoke to them again in parables, saying,
**2** “The kingdom of heaven is like a king who prepared a wedding feast for his son.
**3** He sent his servants to call those invited to the feast, but they refused to come.
**4** Again, he sent other servants, saying, ‘Tell those invited: “Behold, I have prepared my feast; my oxen and fattened cattle are slaughtered; everything is ready. Come to the wedding feast.”’
**5** But they disregarded the invitation; one went to his farm, another to his business.
**6** The others seized his servants, abused them, and killed them.
**7** When the king heard this, he was enraged. He sent his armies, destroyed those murderers, and burned up their city.
**8** Then he said to his servants, ‘The wedding feast is ready, but those invited were not worthy.
**9** So go into the highways and invite everyone you find to the wedding.’
**10** The servants went out into the highways and gathered all they found, both good and bad, and the wedding hall was filled with guests.
**11** But when the king came to see the guests, he noticed a man without a wedding garment.
**12** He said to him, ‘Friend, how did you get in here without a wedding garment?’ The man was speechless.
**13** Then the king said to the servants, ‘Bind him hand and foot and throw him into the outer darkness, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-22-13-a'>a</a></sup>
**14** For many are called, but only a few are chosen.”
### Paying Taxes to Caesar
**15** Then the Pharisees went and plotted how to trap him in his words.
**16** They sent their disciples to him, along with the Herodians, saying, “Teacher, we know you are truthful and teach the way of God with integrity. You are not swayed by others, for you do not show partiality.
**17** So tell us then, what do you think? Is it lawful to pay taxes to Caesar or not?”
**18** But Jesus, aware of their malice, said, “Why are you testing me, you hypocrites?
**19** Show me the coin used for the tax.” So they brought him a <a href="glossary.xhtml#denarius" class="glossary-word">denarius</a>.
**20** He asked them, “Whose image and inscription is this?”
**21** They said, “Caesar's.” Then he said to them, “Give to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's.”
**22** When they heard this, they were amazed, and they left him and went away.
### The Question About the Resurrection
**23** That same day the Sadducees, who say there is no resurrection, came to him and questioned him,
**24** “Teacher,” they said, “Moses said that if a man dies without children, his brother must marry his wife and raise up offspring for his brother.
**25** Now there were seven brothers among us. The first married and died without offspring, so he left his wife to his brother.
**26** The same happened to the second, and then the third, down to the seventh.
**27** Finally, the woman also died.
**28** So in the resurrection, whose wife will she be of the seven? For they all had her as a wife.”
**29** Jesus answered, “You are mistaken, not knowing the Scriptures or the power of God.
**30** For in the resurrection, they neither marry nor are given in marriage, but are like the angels of God in heaven.
**31** But concerning the resurrection of the dead, have you not read what God said to you,
**32** ‘I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob’? He is not the God of the dead, but of the living.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-22-32-b'>b</a></sup>
**33** When the crowds heard this, they were amazed at his teaching.
### The Greatest Commandment
**34** When the Pharisees heard Jesus had silenced the Sadducees, they gathered together.
**35** One of them, an expert in the law, tested him with a question:
**36** “Teacher, which is the greatest commandment in the law?”
**37** Jesus replied, “‘Love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your mind.’
**38** This is the first and greatest commandment.
**39** And the second is like it: ‘Love your neighbor as yourself.’
**40** All the Law and the Prophets are founded on these two commandments.”
### Whose Son Is the Christ?
**41** While the Pharisees were gathered together, Jesus asked them,
**42** “What do you think about the Christ? Whose son is he?”
**43** They replied, “The son of David.”
**44** He said to them, “How then does David, speaking in the Spirit, call him ‘Lord,’ saying,
**45** ‘The Lord said to my Lord, “Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies a footstool for your feet”’?”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-22-45-c'>c</a></sup>
**46** And no one could answer him, and from that day no one dared to question him anymore.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 22:13* - a. The wedding garment in this parable symbolizes the righteousness required to enter the kingdom of heaven, not merely an invitation. In the cultural context, it was customary for a host to provide garments for guests, making the absence of one a sign of rejection or presumption. The phrase “outer darkness” reflects ultimate exclusion from divine presence, characterized by grief and despair. The image of “weeping and gnashing of teeth” further underscores the severity of this separation, capturing profound regret and irreversible loss.
*Matthew 22:32* - b. When Jesus quotes, “I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob,” he draws from Exodus 3:6, where God speaks to Moses from the burning bush. The present tense “I am” signifies that these patriarchs, though physically dead, are alive to God, affirming the ongoing relationship between God and his covenant people beyond death. This declaration directly challenges the Sadducees' denial of the resurrection by revealing that God's promises to the patriarchs remain active, requiring their continued existence. It underscores the eternal nature of God's covenant and the certainty of resurrection, aligning divine identity with the living, not the dead.
*Matthew 22:45* - c. Jesus' question, “If then David calls him ‘Lord,’ how can he be his son?” references Psalm 110:1, a key messianic passage. In Jewish thought, the Messiah was expected to be a descendant of David, a royal son who would restore Israel. However, by addressing the Messiah as “Lord,” David acknowledges a superior, divine authority, revealing a deeper mystery about the Messiah's identity. This implies that the Christ is not merely a human heir but also a divine figure, greater than David and preexistent in authority—a profound revelation that transcends mere lineage and points to the divine nature of the Messiah.

**34** Therefore, I am sending you prophets, wise ones, and <a href="glossary.xhtml#scribes" class="glossary-word">scribes</a>. Some of them you will kill and crucify, others you will flog in your synagogues and pursue from town to town.
**35** As a result, all the righteous blood shed on earth will come upon you, from the blood of righteous Abel to the blood of Zechariah son of Berekiah, whom you murdered between the sanctuary and the altar.
**36** Truly, I tell you, all this will come upon this generation.
### Lament Over Jerusalem
**37** Jerusalem, Jerusalem, the one who kills the prophets and stones those sent to her! How often I have wanted to gather your children together, as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, but you were not willing.
**38** Behold, your house is abandoned to you in utter desolation.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-23-38-c'>c</a></sup>
**39** For I tell you, you will see me no more until you say, ‘Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord.’&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-23-39-d'>d</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 23:23* - a. *These herbs represent small, easily measured tithes, contrasted with the weightier matters of the law like justice, mercy, and faithfulness. Jesus highlights their obsessive focus on minor rituals while neglecting core ethical principles.*
*Matthew 23:27* - b. *Whitewashing tombs was a common practice before Passover to prevent accidental ritual defilement. The imagery symbolizes external purity concealing internal decay and death, capturing the hypocrisy of outward righteousness without true inner transformation.*
*Matthew 23:38* - c. *The term 'house' <i>oikos</i> here can refer to the temple, the city of Jerusalem, or the broader covenant community. The desolation foretold reflects both the physical destruction of the temple (fulfilled in 70 AD) and the spiritual separation resulting from rejecting the Messiah.*
*Matthew 23:39* - d. *The phrase is a direct reference to Psalm 118:26, a messianic psalm often recited during the Passover. This line was famously shouted during Jesus' triumphal entry (Matthew 21:9) and is here used as a prophetic declaration of His eventual recognition as the Messiah at His return.*
## Chapter 24
### Destruction of the Temple
**1** As Jesus left the temple, his disciples drew his attention to the grandeur of its buildings.
**2** But Jesus said to them, “Do you see all these things? Truly, I tell you, not one stone will remain upon another, until it all is torn down.”
### Signs and Warnings
**3** As he sat on the Mount of Olives, the disciples came to him privately and asked, “Tell us, when will these things happen, and what will be the sign of your coming and the end of the age?”
**4** Jesus answered them, “Listen carefully that no one deceives you.
**5** For many will come in my name, claiming, ‘I am the Christ,’ and will deceive many.
**6** You will hear of wars and rumors of wars. Do not be alarmed, for these things are inevitable, but they are not the sign of the end.
**7** For nation will rise against nation, and kingdom against kingdom. There will be famines, plagues, and earthquakes in various places.
**8** All these are just the beginning of birth pains.
**9** Then they will hand you over to be persecuted and killed, and you will be hated by all nations because of my name.
**10** At that time, many will fall away, betray one another, and hate one another.
**11** Many false prophets will arise and spread deception.
**12** Because lawlessness will abound, the love of many will grow cold.
**13** But the one who endures to the end will be saved.
**14** And this gospel of the kingdom will be proclaimed in all the world as a testimony to all nations, and then the end will come.
### The Abomination of Desolation
**15** So when you see the abomination of desolation, spoken of by the prophet Daniel, standing in the holy place (left for the reader's discernment),&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-15-a'>a</a></sup>
**16** then let those in Judea flee to the mountains.
**17** Let the one on the housetop not come down to take anything from the house.
**18** And let the one in the field not turn back to get a cloak.
**19** Grief upon those who are pregnant and nursing in those days!&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-19-b'>b</a></sup>
**20** Pray that your flight is not in winter or on the Sabbath.
**21** For then there will be great tribulation, unmatched from the beginning of the world until now, and never to be repeated.
**22** If those days were not cut short, no one would be saved, but for the sake of the chosen, those days will be shortened.
**23** Then if anyone tells you, ‘Look, here is the Christ!’ or ‘There he is!’ do not believe it.
**24** For false christs and false prophets will arise and perform great signs and wonders to deceive, if they could even reach the chosen.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-24-c'>c</a></sup>
**25** Beware, for I have told you ahead of time.
**26** So if they insist, ‘Look, he is in the wilderness,’ do not go out, or ‘Look, he is in the inner rooms,’ do not believe it.
**27** For just as lightning comes from the east and shines to the west, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.
**28** Wherever the corpse is, there the vultures will circle.
### The Coming of the Son of Man
**29** Immediately after the tribulation of those days, the sun will be darkened, the moon will cease to shine, the stars will fall from the sky, and the powers of the heavens will be shaken.
**30** Then the sign of the Son of Man will appear in the sky, and all the peoples of the earth will mourn, for they will see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of heaven with power and great glory.
**31** And he will send out his angels with a mighty trumpet call, and they will gather his chosen from the four winds, from one end of the heavens to the other.
### The Lesson of the Fig Tree
**32** Learn this lesson from the fig tree: When its branch becomes tender and sprouts leaves, you know that summer is near.
**33** So also, when you see all these things, know that it is near, right at the door.
**34** Truly, I tell you, this age will not pass away until all these things have happened.
**35** Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will never pass away.
### Unknown Day and Hour
**36** But concerning that day and hour, no one knows; not the angels of heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father.
**37** For as it was in the days of Noah, so will it be at the coming of the Son of Man.
**38** For in the days before the flood, people were eating and drinking, taking wives and giving daughters in marriage, until the day Noah entered the ark.
**39** and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all away; so will be the coming of the Son of Man.
**40** Then two will be in the field; one will be taken, and the other left.
**41** Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be taken, and the other left.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-41-d'>d</a></sup>
**42** Therefore, stay alert, for you do not know on what day or hour your Lord will come.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-42-e'>e</a></sup>
**43** But understand this: if the master of the house had known at what time the thief would come, he would have stayed awake and not allowed his house to be broken into.
**44** For this reason, you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not expect.
### The Faithful and Wicked Slave
**45** Who then is the faithful and wise slave, whom the master has put in charge of the household to give them their food at the proper time?&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-45-f'>f</a></sup>
**46** Blessed is that slave whom the master finds doing his will when he returns.
**47** Truly, I tell you, they will be put in charge of all the master's possessions.
**48** But if that wicked slave says to themselves, ‘My master is delayed,’
**49** and begins to beat their fellow slaves and eat and drink with the drunkards,
**50** the master of that slave will come on a day they do not expect and at an hour they do not know,
**51** and will cut them to pieces and send them to their place with the hypocrites, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-24-51-g'>g</a></sup>

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 24:15* - a. The phrase “left for the reader's discernment” suggests that the reference to the “abomination of desolation,” spoken of by the prophet Daniel, is a prophetic sign requiring careful reflection. It likely refers to a specific historical event, such as the desecration of the temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 168 B.C. or the Roman destruction in 70 A.D., but it also hints at a broader, recurring fulfillment, encouraging each generation to discern its meaning in their own context.
*Matthew 24:19* - b. The phrase “Grief upon those who are pregnant and nursing in those days” emphasizes the extreme vulnerability and unavoidable suffering that such individuals will face during the coming tribulation. This is not a judgment against them personally, but a recognition of the harsh conditions that will make escape and survival particularly challenging for those burdened with the care of the most dependent. It captures the intensity of the crisis, when even the strongest natural bonds and instincts for protection will be strained to the breaking point.
*Matthew 24:24* - c. The phrase “if they could even reach the chosen” acknowledges that the deception will be so intense and persuasive that it could nearly mislead even those specifically chosen by God. This phrasing captures the severity of the spiritual threat without asserting that the chosen can actually be overcome, reflecting a divine safeguard over those called to endure. However, this does not imply immunity from testing; the chosen will still face extreme pressure, falsehoods, and trials designed to shake even the firmest faith. Their perseverance will be both a mark of divine preservation and a testament to their steadfast loyalty.
*Matthew 24:41* - d. The image of “two women grinding at the mill” reflects a common, daily task in the ancient world, typically performed by women using a hand mill or stone quern. This phrase emphasizes the sudden, selective nature of judgment, where ordinary life is interrupted without warning, dividing even those engaged in the same work. It highlights the unpredictability of the coming separation and the need for constant readiness.
*Matthew 24:42* - e. The phrase “on what day or hour” captures the full uncertainty of the timing, emphasizing that even those most vigilant cannot anticipate the exact moment. This reflects the broader biblical theme of spiritual vigilance, as seen in the parable of the ten virgins (Matthew 25), where readiness is essential for those awaiting the bridegroom's return.
*Matthew 24:45* - f. The choice of the term “slave” rather than “servant” reflects the deeper theological reality that those who follow Christ are considered bondservants or slaves to their master, Jesus. This aligns with the broader New Testament teaching that believers are purchased by Christ's sacrifice and belong fully to him, serving his will above their own. This concept is emphasized in passages like 1 Corinthians 6:19-20 (“You are not your own; you were bought with a price”) and Romans 6:22 (“But now, having been freed from sin and enslaved to God, you derive your benefit, resulting in sanctification, and the outcome, eternal life”). This terminology captures the full scope of devotion and obedience expected of true followers.
*Matthew 24:51* - g. The phrase “send them to their place with the hypocrites” reflects the severe and final nature of the judgment described. The Greek here, <i>meros</i>, carries the sense of an appointed portion or rightful place, emphasizing that the wicked slave receives what is fitting for their unfaithfulness. This aligns with the broader biblical principle that those who betray their master's trust will face a just and severe recompense, often characterized by separation from the faithful and eternal regret, as seen in the repeated phrase “weeping and gnashing of teeth” throughout Matthew (e.g., 8:12, 13:42, 13:50, 22:13, 25:30).
## Chapter 25
### The Parable of the Ten Bridesmaids
**1** Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten bridesmaids who took their lamps and went out to meet the bridegroom.
**2** Five of them were wise, and five were foolish.
**3** The foolish ones took their lamps but took no extra oil.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-25-3-a'>a</a></sup>
**4** But the wise took oil in jars along with their lamps.
**5** When the bridegroom delayed, they all became drowsy and fell asleep.
**6** But at midnight there was a shout: “Look! The bridegroom is coming; go out to meet him!”
**7** Then all the bridesmaids rose and trimmed their lamps.
**8** The foolish said to the wise, “Give us some of your oil, for our lamps are going out.”
**9** But the wise replied, “No, there may not be enough for both us and you. Instead, go to those who sell oil and buy some for yourselves.”
**10** But while they were on their way to buy oil, the bridegroom arrived. Those who were ready went in with him to the wedding feast, and the door was shut.
**11** Later, the other bridesmaids came also, saying, “Lord, Lord, open the door for us!”
**12** But he replied, “Truly, I tell you, I do not know you.”
**13** Therefore, keep watch, for you do not know the day or the hour in which the Son of Man is coming.
### The Parable of the Talents
**14** For it is like a man going on a journey, who called his slaves and entrusted his possessions to them.
**15** To one he gave five talents, to another two, and to another one, each according to their ability; then he went on his journey.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-25-15-b'>b</a></sup>
**16** The one who had received five talents went at once and invested them, gaining five more.
**17** Similarly, the one who had received two gained two more.
**18** But the one who had received one went away, dug a hole in the ground, and buried his master’s money.
**19** After a long time, the master of those slaves returned to settle accounts with them.
**20** The one who had received five talents came and brought five more, saying, “Master, you entrusted me with five talents; see, I have gained five more.”
**21** His master replied, “Well done, good and faithful slave. You have been faithful over a few things; I will put you in charge of many things. Enter into the joy of your master.”
**22** The one with two talents also came and said, “Master, you entrusted me with two talents; see, I have gained two more.”
**23** His master replied, “Well done, good and faithful slave. You have been faithful over a few things; I will put you in charge of many things. Enter into the joy of your master.”
**24** Then the one who had received the one <a href="glossary.xhtml#talent" class="glossary-word">talent</a> came and said, “Master, I knew you were a hard man, reaping where you did not sow and gathering where you did not scatter seed.”
**25** “So I was afraid and went and hid your <a href="glossary.xhtml#talent" class="glossary-word">talent</a> in the ground. See, you have what is yours.”
**26** But his master replied, “You wicked and lazy slave! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I have not scattered.”
**27** Then you should have deposited my money with the bankers, so that when I returned, I would have received it back with interest.
**28** So take the <a href="glossary.xhtml#talent" class="glossary-word">talent</a> from him and give it to the one who has ten talents.
**29** For to everyone who has, more will be given, and they will have an abundance. But from the one who has not, even what they have will be taken away.
**30** And banish the worthless slave into the outer darkness, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-25-30-c'>c</a></sup>
### The Final Judgment
**31** When the Son of Man comes in his glory, and all the angels with him, then he will sit on the throne of his glory.
**32** And all the nations will be gathered before him, and he will separate them one from another, as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats.
**33** And he will place the sheep on his right and the goats on his left.
**34** Then the King will say to those on his right, “Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world.
**35** For I was hungry and you gave me food, I was thirsty and you gave me drink, I was a stranger and you took me in,
**36** I was naked and you clothed me, I was sick and you visited me, I was in prison and you came to me.”
**37** Then the righteous will answer, “Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you, or thirsty and give you drink?
**38** When did we see you a stranger and take you in, or naked and clothe you?
**39** When did we see you sick or in prison and come to you?”
**40** And the King will reply, “Truly, I tell you, whatever you did for one of the least of my beloved, you did for me.”&#8239;<sup><a href='#matthew-25-40-d'>d</a></sup>
**41** Then he will say to those on his left, “Depart from me, you who are cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels.
**42** For I was hungry and you gave me no food, I was thirsty and you gave me no drink,
**43** I was a stranger and you did not take me in, naked and you did not clothe me, sick and in prison and you did not visit me.”
**44** Then they also will answer, “Lord, when did we see you hungry or thirsty or a stranger or naked or sick or in prison, and did not serve you?”
**45** Then he will reply, “Truly, I tell you, whatever you did not do for one of the least of my beloved, you did not do for me.”
**46** And they will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life.

#### Translation Notes
*Matthew 25:3* - a. In first-century Jewish life, olive oil was crucial for daily living and especially for lighting lamps during weddings and night processions. Running out of oil signified unpreparedness for the bridegroom’s arrival. In this parable, oil represents a life of vigilant, cultivated readiness for Christ’s return, echoing prophetic calls to spiritual alertness.
*Matthew 25:15* - b. The Greek <i>talanton</i> was a unit of weight (around 33–36 kg) used for silver or gold. As currency, a talent equaled about 6,000 denarii—roughly 20 years’ wages for a laborer—highlighting the enormous responsibility entrusted in the parable.
*Matthew 25:30* - c. “Outer darkness” is a distinctive phrase in Matthew, evoking banishment from the festive banquet of God’s kingdom (see also 8:12; 22:13). This image draws on Second Temple themes of exclusion from divine presence, intensified by the phrase “weeping and gnashing of teeth”—a formula for deep anguish and irreversible loss at final judgment.
*Matthew 25:40* - d. The phrase “the least of my beloved” is a rendering of the Greek ‘the least of these my brothers (<i>adelphoi</i>)’. It identifies the recipients as Christ’s own people—his disciples or followers. By calling them 'beloved', the translation echoes Old Testament covenant language and highlights that serving Christ’s people is regarded as serving Christ himself (cf. Matthew 10:40–42; Proverbs 19:17).
