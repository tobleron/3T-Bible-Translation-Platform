for it is written (default for conciseness) vs for it has been written
for vs because (use for when causation is mainly for poetic elegance and because when there is logical argument).

All this happened (default more modern) vs All this came to pass.

"to fulfil what the Lord had spoken through the prophet saying" vs "that it might be fulfilled which was spoken by the Lord through the prophet saying"
(This concise phrasing maintains poetic tone while conveying the fulfillment of prophecy clearly and directly. It is more accessible than the alternative phrasing which is more cumbersome.)

use I also vs Me too (more poetic elegance and less fluffy)

use no exclamation marks
use speak for divine special occasion revelations and tell for casual messages and easier readability
use deeply as emotion intensifier instead of greatly to avoid negative connotations attributed to greatness
use surrounding region instead of boundaries
use stylistic choice to move word saying to the end of the previous verse rather than the beginning of the first verse.

Greek Verbs and Suitable English Renderings:

κηρύσσω (kērussō) – "preach"
Verses: Matt 3:1, Mark 1:4, Luke 3:3, Luke 3:18, Matt 4:23, Acts 8:4, Acts 10:36, Rom 10:15

καταγγέλλω (kataggellō) – "proclaim"
Verses: Acts 16:17, Acts 17:3, Acts 17:18, Acts 17:23

Use “preaching” when translating κηρύσσω, which means to herald or authoritatively announce a divine message—especially in gospel, repentance, or prophetic contexts. Use “proclaiming” for καταγγέλλω, which emphasizes declarative or formal announcement, often in public reasoning or courtroom-like settings (e.g., Paul in Athens).
